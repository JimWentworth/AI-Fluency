Move from “search reflex” to disciplined collaboration

This site turns your text into practice. Each tab includes a short framing plus activities and copy/paste prompts you can run in ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Copilot, or Illinois Chat.

Today: February 17, 2026 • Tip: For workshops, do the activities in pairs—one “driver” and one “verifier.”

How to use

Do one exercise, then debrief

The point is not to get a perfect answer. The point is to practice steering: clarifying intent, adding constraints, asking for alternatives, and checking what matters.

Workshop move: stop calling it “prompting”
In an academic setting, “prompting” can sound like a trick. Try “iterative specification” or “draft + critique loop.” It sets the expectation that humans remain accountable.
Ground rule

Separate usefulness from truth

Generative systems can be excellent for ideation and drafting while still being unreliable for factual claims. Build the habit of deciding what kind of output you’re asking for.

1
Is this a drafting/idea task or a fact/decision task?
2
What would count as evidence the output is acceptable for your context?

1) The Search Engine Reflex → The Conversation Model

Many of us learned technology through search engines: compress the need into keywords, expect a single-turn answer, then judge success by the top result. When people open ChatGPT/Claude/Gemini, they often repeat that pattern. That’s not misuse. It’s muscle memory.

Graphic

Search query vs iterative conversation

Use this as a quick visual explanation: search optimizes retrieval; generative tools optimize drafting under direction.

Workshop prompt: Ask participants to describe what “success” means in each model. In conversation, success often requires a second move.
Key shift

From command → dialogue

Treat the first output as a draft that is waiting for your direction. Your follow-up message is where the leverage is.

Micro-skill: specify one constraint at a time
Try changing only one variable per turn (audience, tone, length, evidence standard). You’ll learn what actually controls the output.
Activity

Search-style vs iterative framing

Run both prompts below. Then compare: which one forces the model to wait for your direction instead of guessing your intent?

Copy/paste — Search-style expectation 
Write a two-paragraph explanation of dark matter.
Copy/paste — Iterative framing 
Give me an initial draft explaining dark matter for a high school audience.
I will tell you what to adjust after you draft it.
At the end, ask me 3 clarification questions that would improve the next revision.
Debrief questions
Which output made assumptions about your goals? Which one invited you into the process? If the first draft is “fine,” what makes it not yet “yours”?
Exercise

Three-step refinement loop (fast)

Start with a draft. Then do two rapid iterations: (1) constraint, (2) verification posture.

Copy/paste — Step 1: initial draft 
Draft a short announcement for a University of Illinois audience about a generative AI workshop.
Keep it welcoming and specific. End with: “Reply with questions.”
Copy/paste — Step 2: add one constraint 
Revise with one constraint: remove buzzwords and avoid hype.
Keep it friendly but concrete.
Copy/paste — Step 3: verification posture 
Now list anything in the announcement that would require verification (dates, claims, policies).
For each item, suggest what source I should check.

Core claim: generative systems aren’t “search with better grammar.” They’re drafting collaborators that need direction.

2) “Usable” vs “Correct” • Feedback • Branching

Generative systems predict plausible continuations. That makes them strong at drafts, outlines, options, and reframes—but unreliable as sources of accuracy. A productive reframe is to ask: not “Is this correct?”, but “Is this usable for my next step?”

Jump to:
Reframe

What does “usable” mean here?

“Usable” depends on the task. A brainstorming list can be 70% right and still be valuable. A policy claim needs citations.

Quick rubric (in prose)
Ask whether the output (a) reduces blank-page friction, (b) fits your audience and constraints, and (c) makes its uncertainty visible when needed. If it can’t do (c), treat it as a draft—not a source.
Feedback

Buttons are weak; follow-ups are strong

Thumbs up/down can help steer over time, but your real leverage is the follow-up message you write: redirect the approach, add constraints, or ask for alternatives.

If you only say “wrong,” the model learns nothing actionable. If you say “too generic—challenge my assumptions and offer two unconventional angles,” you’ve given it a job.
Activity 1

Turn a mediocre answer into a better one using feedback

Use a topic from your unit. First ask for a generic answer. Then steer it with a directional follow-up that changes stance, depth, or audience.

Copy/paste — Generate a generic baseline 
Give me a general explanation of how generative AI impacts higher education.
Copy/paste — Directional feedback (make it useful) 
This is too generic. Re-approach it by:
1) challenging two common assumptions faculty have about generative AI,
2) offering two unconventional but plausible angles,
3) writing for a mixed UIUC audience (faculty, staff, students) with no hype.
End with 3 questions I should ask my unit before adopting any AI workflow.
Debrief prompt: separate “better writing” from “better thinking”
Did the revision improve substance, or just tone? Which parts would require evidence? What would you keep if you were writing this without AI?
Branching

Split the thread on purpose

Long chats accumulate inertia: the model carries old assumptions forward. Branching gives you parallel prototypes without contaminating the main thread.

When to branch (rule)
If your next question would reshape the assumptions, framing, or goals, branch. If it’s a refinement within the same goal, stay in the thread.
Activity 2

Branching drill: two directions, same starting point

Start with one draft, then branch into two versions: one aimed at “efficiency,” one aimed at “risk control.”

Copy/paste — Shared starting point 
Draft a one-page guidance note for a university unit on using generative AI responsibly.
Keep it practical and readable.
Branch A: efficiency posture
Copy/paste — Branch A 
Rewrite the guidance with an “efficiency-first” posture.
Focus on quick wins and low-friction practices.
Still include a short section on what not to do.
Branch B: risk-control posture
Copy/paste — Branch B 
Rewrite the guidance with a “risk-control” posture.
Prioritize privacy, accuracy, equity, accessibility, and policy alignment.
Add a short section on verification practices.
Activity 3

Ask for “what might be missing”

If you only ask for answers, you get answers. If you ask for gaps, you get a thinking partner.

Copy/paste — Missingness prompt 
Here is my draft plan: [paste].
What might be missing that could cause failure in a university context?
Group missing items into: stakeholders, constraints, evidence, and risks.
Then ask me 5 questions that would clarify those gaps.

Core claim: iteration is not surrender. It’s editorial control. You still own relevance, ethics, and correctness.

3) Human Judgment, Disciplinary Standards, and Academic Integrity

Generative AI can widen possibilities and reorganize chaos into patterns, but it’s still a prediction engine—not an authority, not a subject-matter expert, and not a decision-maker. Productive use depends on your discipline and your ability to evaluate the output.

Use AI when the task is…

Generative, organizational, comparative, low-consequence

AI excels when you need ideas, variation, options, counter-approaches, alternative framings, or help structuring content you already understand.

Practice: make AI stay in its lane
Ask it for multiple versions and to label uncertainty. Then you decide what belongs, what is ethical, and what requires verification.
Stick to humans when the task is…

Context-dependent, ethically consequential, fact-heavy, decision work

If the work requires deep institutional knowledge, affects students or policy, or depends on accurate claims, the human should lead.

Rule of thumb: Use AI only in domains where you can independently verify the output or where the stakes of getting it wrong are low.
Academic integrity

Not “Was AI used?” but “What was this work supposed to demonstrate?”

AI complicates integrity because it sits between thinking and doing. The key is whether AI performed the thinking or judgment the task was meant to assess or develop.

Scenario exercise (small group)
Choose one scenario below. Decide: acceptable use, unacceptable use, or “depends.” Then name what disclosure would look like.
A
A student uses AI to brainstorm project topics, then writes their own proposal.
B
A student pastes a prompt and submits the generated essay as-is.
C
An instructor uses AI to draft rubric language, then revises it based on course outcomes.
D
A staff member asks AI to interpret a policy and acts on it without checking the source.
Decision drill

Three gating questions (fast)

Use these before adopting any AI workflow in a unit or course.

Copy/paste — Gating questions 
I am considering using AI for this task: [describe task].
Answer these questions:
1) Can I independently verify the output without specialized research? If not, what’s the risk?
2) Would an error materially harm a person, policy, equity, accessibility, privacy, or institutional credibility?
3) Am I asking AI to generate something—or to decide something?

Then recommend: use AI, use AI with constraints, or do not use AI.
Explain the reasoning plainly, and list what evidence I should consult if I proceed.
Discipline anchoring

Make the tool reflect your standards

Different fields have different norms for evidence, citation, uncertainty, and acceptable approximation. Tell the model what your discipline requires.

Copy/paste — “Disciplinary standard” prompt 
Use the standards of my discipline: [name discipline or context].
When you answer:
- separate claims from evidence,
- label uncertainty,
- avoid invented citations,
- and suggest how I would verify any factual assertion.
Now help me draft: [task].

Core claim: AI widens possibilities; humans decide validity, ethics, and alignment with institutional and disciplinary standards.
