2. Iterative Prompting: The Core Technique

‚ñº

Iterative prompting means treating your interaction with AI as a multi-turn conversation where each exchange builds on the last, progressively refining outputs toward your actual needs.

The Four-Part Pattern

1. Start Broad, Then Narrow

Begin with exploratory questions that help you map the territory before committing to a specific direction.

Example: Designing a Course Activity

Initial Prompt:
"I'm teaching a course on environmental policy and want students to understand the complexity of stakeholder interests in climate decisions. What are some pedagogical approaches that help students navigate multiple competing perspectives?"
Follow-up Prompt (After Reviewing Options):
"The role-play simulation approach is interesting. How might I structure that for a 50-minute class with 25 students where the topic is local renewable energy adoption?"
Refinement Prompt:
"I like the stakeholder role assignment, but I'm concerned students might not have enough background knowledge. Could you suggest how to scaffold their preparation without overwhelming them before class?"

2. Ask for Alternatives

Don't settle for the first suggestion. Requesting multiple approaches helps you make informed choices rather than accepting defaults.

Example: Assessment Design

After Receiving Initial Suggestion:
"This essay approach could work, but I'm wondering about alternatives. Can you suggest three different assessment formats that would evaluate the same learning outcomes but engage different student strengths?"

3. Challenge and Probe

Push back on AI suggestions. This isn't about being difficult‚Äîit's about stress-testing ideas before implementing them.

Example: Challenging an Activity Design

Critical Follow-up:
"I'm concerned this activity might privilege students who are comfortable with verbal debate. What evidence exists about whether this approach creates equity issues? How might I adapt it to be more inclusive?"

4. Request Specificity

Move from general frameworks to concrete, actionable details that you can actually implement.

Example: From Abstract to Concrete

Specificity Request:
"Can you provide the exact instructions I would give students for this activity, including the timing breakdown for each phase and what they should produce by the end?"

Practice: The Five-Prompt Challenge

Choose a teaching challenge you're currently facing. Engage with an AI tool using exactly five prompts, following this structure:

  1. Prompt 1: Describe your challenge broadly and ask for possible approaches
  2. Prompt 2: Select one approach and ask for alternatives or variations
  3. Prompt 3: Challenge an assumption or identify a potential problem
  4. Prompt 4: Request specific, actionable details
  5. Prompt 5: Ask how to adapt it for your specific context

Reflection Questions

  • How did the output from Prompt 5 differ from Prompt 1?
  • At what point did you feel you were getting genuinely useful information?
  • What surprised you about the iterative process?
  • What would have happened if you'd stopped after the first prompt?

3. Knowing When AI Supports vs. When Expertise Must Lead

‚ñº
The goal isn't to use AI for everything‚Äîit's to use AI strategically for tasks that benefit from broad pattern recognition while reserving your expertise for judgment, nuance, and context.

AI as Support: Where It Excels

Generating Possibilities

AI can quickly surface multiple options you might not have considered, expanding your solution space.

Example: "What are ten different ways I could help students practice statistical reasoning outside of problem sets?"

Identifying Patterns Across Domains

AI can connect ideas from different fields, offering cross-disciplinary perspectives.

Example: "How do other fields beyond education approach the challenge of helping learners transfer knowledge to new contexts?"

Drafting and Structuring

AI can create initial frameworks that you then refine with your disciplinary expertise.

Example: "Draft an outline for a lesson introducing the concept of systemic risk, including key questions students should be able to answer."

Playing Devil's Advocate

AI can surface counterarguments or alternative perspectives.

Example: "What are the strongest critiques of this pedagogical approach from different theoretical perspectives?"

Human Expertise Must Lead: Where You're Essential

Critical Judgment Zones

These areas require human expertise and shouldn't be delegated to AI:

  • Disciplinary Accuracy: AI can hallucinate facts, especially in specialized domains
  • Contextual Appropriateness: Only you know your specific students, institutional culture, and constraints
  • Ethical Implications: AI can't evaluate the ethical dimensions of pedagogical choices
  • Cultural Sensitivity: AI may miss cultural nuances or perpetuate biases
  • Learning Science: AI doesn't replace understanding of how learning actually works

Example: When Expertise Must Lead

Scenario: Designing a discussion activity about a sensitive historical topic.

What AI Can Do: Suggest discussion formats, provide historical background, propose question sequences.

What Only You Can Do:

  • Evaluate whether the framing is appropriate for your students' backgrounds
  • Anticipate which aspects might be traumatic for particular students
  • Determine how this fits with your course's larger narrative
  • Assess whether the sources suggested are appropriate and credible
  • Make real-time adjustments based on classroom dynamics

Decision Framework

Use AI When... Lead with Expertise When...
You need to explore many options quickly Deep disciplinary knowledge is required
You're looking for creative alternatives Student-specific context matters critically
You need a structural framework to refine Ethical judgment is central to the decision
You want cross-disciplinary perspectives Accuracy is paramount and you can verify
You're stuck and need to break through Cultural sensitivity is essential
You need help articulating tacit knowledge Assessment of student learning is at stake

4. Exploratory Processes, Not Shortcuts

‚ñº

One of the biggest risks in using GenAI is treating it as a shortcut‚Äîa way to skip thinking rather than enhance it. The distinction is crucial.

The Shortcut Trap

Warning Signs You're Using AI as a Shortcut

  • You accept the first output without critical evaluation
  • You're relieved you don't have to think deeply about the problem
  • You couldn't explain the reasoning behind the AI's suggestions
  • You're using AI to avoid learning something you actually need to understand
  • You feel like you're "getting away with something"

The Exploration Mindset

Characteristics of Genuine Exploration

  • Curiosity-Driven: You're genuinely interested in understanding the problem space more deeply
  • Questioning Stance: You regularly challenge AI outputs and ask "why?"
  • Knowledge Building: Each interaction increases your understanding, not just your outputs
  • Integration: You're connecting AI insights with your existing expertise
  • Improved Judgment: Your ability to evaluate options gets sharper, not duller

Comparison: Shortcut vs. Exploration

Scenario: Designing a New Course Assignment

Shortcut Approach:

"Create an assignment for my environmental science course on ecosystem services. Make it appropriate for juniors."

Exploratory Approach:

"I want students to understand ecosystem services beyond just memorizing definitions. What are different ways this concept is taught across disciplines?"
"The project-based approach is interesting. What are examples of how this has been implemented? What challenges did instructors encounter?"
"I'm concerned about equity issues with field-based projects. How have others addressed this? What are alternative ways to achieve the same learning outcomes?"

    5. Expanding Ideas While Maintaining Agency

    ‚ñº

    The promise of AI collaboration is cognitive bandwidth expansion‚Äîthe ability to explore more possibilities, consider more angles, and think through more scenarios than you could alone. But this only works if you maintain intellectual agency.

    What Does "Maintaining Agency" Mean?

    Agency in AI Collaboration

    You maintain agency when you:

    • Set the direction and goals of the exploration
    • Evaluate AI suggestions against your expertise and values
    • Make final decisions based on your judgment
    • Understand the reasoning behind suggestions well enough to defend or critique them
    • Feel ownership over the final product

    Signs You're Losing Agency

    • Feeling like the AI is "in charge" of the direction
    • Accepting suggestions you don't fully understand
    • Inability to explain why you made certain choices
    • Feeling dependent on AI for decisions you used to make confidently
    • Anxiety about whether the output is "good enough" without being able to judge it yourself

    Strategies for Maintaining Agency

    1. Start with Your Own Thinking

    Before engaging AI, articulate what you already know and what you're trying to achieve. This anchors the conversation in your expertise.

    Practice This

    Before your next AI interaction, write 2-3 sentences:

    • What do I already know about this problem?
    • What am I trying to accomplish?
    • What would success look like?

    2. Actively Evaluate Every Suggestion

    Don't passively receive AI outputs. Actively judge each suggestion against criteria that matter to you.

    Evaluation Questions

    • Does this align with my pedagogical values?
    • Is this feasible given my constraints?
    • What assumptions is this suggestion making?
    • What could go wrong with this approach?
    • How would my students experience this?

    3. Synthesize, Don't Transcribe

    Your goal is to integrate AI insights with your own thinking, not copy outputs verbatim.

    Synthesis Practice

    After an AI interaction, write a brief reflection:

    • What new perspectives did I gain?
    • What confirmed my existing thinking?
    • What do I want to adapt and why?
    • What am I choosing to reject and why?

    4. Use AI to Expand, Not Replace, Your Thinking

    The goal is to consider more possibilities than you could alone, not to outsource decision-making.

    Expansion Prompts

    • "What am I not considering?"
    • "What would someone with a different perspective say?"
    • "What are alternative approaches I might not have thought of?"
    • "What could go wrong with my current plan?"
