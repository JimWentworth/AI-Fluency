Iterative Prompting as Structured Exploration

Iteration as a Deliberate Practice

Iterative prompting is not about “fixing a bad answer” so much as sculpting a line of inquiry. You begin with a rough prompt, examine what the model gives you, and then adjust your instructions, constraints, or questions based on what is missing or promising.

Core pattern: Explore → Critique → Refine → Apply.

Designing an Iterative Loop

1. Start with a rough draft prompt

The goal of the first prompt is not perfection, but to get something concrete you can react to. 

Here is my context:
[brief description].

Give me an initial set of ideas or approaches.
Do NOT aim for completeness, just get us started.
2. Diagnose what is missing or off-target

Instead of accepting or rejecting the output, ask: What is useful here? What is missing, simplistic, or misaligned with my goals? 

From your last response:
- What seems generic or boilerplate?
- What assumptions are you making about my context?
Suggest revisions that make the response more specific to my situation.
3. Refine with targeted follow-up prompts

Each iteration should be intentional, narrowing or deepening in a particular direction rather than starting from scratch. 

Narrow the focus to:
[specify dimension].

Keep only the most relevant ideas, then deepen each using:
- concrete examples,
- potential pitfalls, and
- questions I should ask before implementing.
4. Apply, then loop back

After trying a small real-world application (e.g., drafting a paragraph, designing a slide, outlining a lesson), you can return to the model with observations. 

Here’s what happened when I tried one of the ideas:
[brief summary].

Help me analyze:
- what worked,
- what didn’t, and
- how I might iterate again.

Prompt Patterns for Structured Exploration

Pattern: Diverge → Converge 
DIVERGE:
Generate many different options, framings, or angles on this topic.

CONVERGE:
From that list, select the 3 that best fit:
[my constraints].

Explain why these 3 fit better than the others.
Pattern: Zoom Out → Zoom In 
ZOOM OUT:
Explain how this issue fits into a larger system:
[system or context].

ZOOM IN:
Now focus on the piece that is most under my control.
Provide actionable steps I could take in the next 30 days.
Pattern: Generate → Stress-Test 
GENERATE:
Propose a solution or plan for:
[describe problem].

STRESS-TEST:
Identify where this solution is likely to fail, and with which stakeholders.
Suggest modifications that make the plan more robust.

Micro-Activity: Iterative Prompting on a Real Task

Choose a real task you are currently facing (designing a session, drafting an email, framing a research question). Use four iterations: 

1) Initial prompt and output.
2) Critique the output and ask for a more specific revision.
3) Ask for alternatives or counter-approaches.
4) Ask for a synthesis tailored to your constraints and voice.

Capture each step so you can later show students or colleagues how your thinking evolved with the model instead of being replaced by it.
