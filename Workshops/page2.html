When AI Supports vs. Expertise Must Lead

Why the Division of Labor Matters

Generative AI is not a junior colleague who can “do everything, just a bit worse.” It is very strong at some tasks and unreliable or even dangerous at others. Effective collaboration requires a conscious division of labor: using AI where pattern breadth helps, and insisting on human expertise where judgment, ethics, or lived context are non-negotiable.

Guiding question: “Is this a pattern-recognition problem, or a judgment/commitment problem?”

Areas Where AI Typically Supports Well

Pattern surfacing and option generation

AI is effective at listing possibilities, clusters, or approaches you might not have considered yet. 

Given this scenario:
[briefly describe],
generate a range of lenses or approaches I could take.
Label each approach as low-risk, medium-risk, or high-risk, and explain why.
Reframing, summarizing, and scaffolding

When you already understand the material, AI can help re-express it for different audiences or levels. 

Here is a concept I understand:
[paste explanation].

Reframe it for:
1) a first-year undergraduate,
2) a graduate seminar, and
3) a campus administrator.

For each, identify what is emphasized and what must be left out.
Surface-level critique and quick “first pass” checks

AI can point out missing sections, confusing structure, or potential audience mismatches, which you then evaluate.

Areas Where Human Expertise Must Lead

High-stakes decisions and ethical commitments

Decisions that affect people’s lives, grades, careers, or safety cannot be delegated to a probabilistic system trained on unknown data. 

You are my brainstorming partner, not a decision-maker.
Help me outline the ethical considerations and possible impacts of this decision on different stakeholders.
Do NOT recommend a final decision, only surface relevant factors.
Interpretation of evidence and local context

AI can suggest interpretations but lacks your positionality, institutional realities, and tacit knowledge. It should never be treated as an authority on “what is actually happening” in your environment.

Assessment, evaluation, and grading

AI can assist in generating criteria or example feedback, but responsibility for actual evaluation should remain human, with transparency to students and colleagues.

Decision Heuristics: When to Use AI, When to Hold Back

Quick decision rule of thumb 
If the task is:
- exploratory,
- low-stakes, and
- mainly about generating or organizing ideas,
then AI is likely appropriate.

If the task is:
- consequential,
- involves real people, or
- depends on nuanced local knowledge or values,
then AI should be advisory at most, and human judgment must lead.
Scenario Exercise

Activity: For each scenario, decide: AI supports, or expertise must lead?

1) Drafting three alternative assignment prompts for a new course.
2) Deciding whether a student’s work violates academic integrity policies.
3) Brainstorming possible workshop titles for a faculty event.
4) Interpreting student course evaluations in a politically sensitive environment.
5) Generating examples to illustrate a complex concept in your discipline.

For each, note why you chose AI support vs. human leadership, and what risks you’re mitigating.
