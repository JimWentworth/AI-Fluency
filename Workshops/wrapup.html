Module 2 Wrap-Up

Knowledge Check: Can You Apply the Core Ideas?

Use the questions below as a self-check. Expand each item to compare your thinking with suggested responses. These can be used in-class as discussion prompts or adapted into quizzes.

1. Input/Output vs. Inquiry

Question: In one or two sentences, explain the difference between using AI as an input/output tool and using it as an inquiry partner.

Suggested answer:

Using AI as an input/output tool means asking for a single answer and accepting it at face value.
Using AI as an inquiry partner means engaging in a back-and-forth process to explore assumptions,
surface alternatives, and iteratively refine ideas while I remain responsible for judgment.
2. When Should Human Expertise Lead?

Question: Describe one example from your own context where AI can help, and one where AI should be strictly advisory or not used at all.

Suggested answer (structure):

Helpful use:
- Example where AI broadens options, drafts alternatives, or reframes ideas
- Task is exploratory or low-stakes

Human must lead:
- Example involving grading, ethics, personnel decisions, or sensitive student data
- Task is high-stakes, context-specific, or value-laden
3. Iterative Prompting

Question: Outline the basic steps in an iterative prompting loop you might use for a real task (e.g., designing an assignment, drafting communication, or planning a workshop).

Suggested answer:

1) Provide context and ask for an initial draft or set of ideas.
2) Diagnose what is useful, missing, or misaligned.
3) Ask for revision that narrows or deepens in a specific direction.
4) Request alternatives or counter-approaches for comparison.
5) Synthesize and adapt the output in my own words for my actual context.
4. Cognitive Bandwidth vs. Outsourcing Judgment

Question: What does it mean to expand cognitive bandwidth without outsourcing judgment? Give a short example.

Suggested answer:

Expanding cognitive bandwidth means using AI to generate more ideas, perspectives, or examples than I could alone,
while I still decide what is accurate, ethical, and appropriate to use. For example, AI can draft many activity ideas
for a course, but I choose which ones to implement and how to adapt them to my students.

Reflection: How Will You Work with AI?

Use the prompts below for written reflection or group discussion. These can also be embedded into a Canvas assignment or journal activity.

Personal Practice Reflection 
- Where in your current work do you most often default to “input/output” interactions with AI?
- How could you redesign one of those interactions as an inquiry-based conversation?
- What boundaries do you want to set for yourself about when AI is not appropriate?
Teaching and Mentoring Reflection 
- How will you explain iterative collaboration with AI to your students or colleagues?
- What guardrails do you want to put in place so that AI supports, rather than replaces, learning?
- What is one concrete activity or assignment you might redesign using the patterns from this module?
Equity and Responsibility Reflection 
- How might AI tools amplify existing inequities if used uncritically in your context?
- How can iterative collaboration (with explicit human judgment) help mitigate some of those risks?
- What will you need from your department or institution to use AI responsibly?
