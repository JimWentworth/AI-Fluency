Tool Highlight: Google Gemini in the Browser

Google’s general-purpose AI assistant, accessible at gemini.google.com.

In this walkthrough, you’ll go from logging in with your Illinois-linked Google account, to using Gemini in the browser for chat, drafting, and light research, with an eye toward how it complements NotebookLM.

What Gemini is (and how it differs from NotebookLM)

Gemini is Google’s general-purpose AI assistant. In the browser, it lives at gemini.google.com and can help with writing, planning, brainstorming, coding, and answering questions, often using up-to-date web information.:contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}

  • Gemini: a broad, web-connected assistant that can chat, generate, and reason across many tasks. It can connect to your Google Workspace (Gmail, Drive, Docs, etc.) when enabled by your institution.:contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}
  • NotebookLM: a “notebook” that’s grounded only in the sources you load into each notebook, ideal for deep reading and study of a defined corpus.:contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}
Campus lens

In this certificate, a useful mental model is: Gemini = fast generalist + web, NotebookLM = focused researcher + your sources. You’ll likely use both, but for different jobs.

1
Sign in at gemini.google.com
  1. Open a browser and go to https://gemini.google.com. :contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}
  2. Click Sign in. Use your Illinois-linked Google account if your institution provides one (for example, NetID@illinois.edu through Google Workspace for Education).
  3. Complete any SSO / multi-factor steps as prompted. You should land on the Gemini home screen with a large prompt box and suggested starters.
  4. If Gemini for Workspace isn’t enabled on your campus account, you may see a message that certain features are unavailable or that you need an eligible subscription. In that case, you can either:
    • Check campus documentation or IT for status, or
    • Use a personal Google account for low-risk, non-sensitive tasks only.
    :contentReference[oaicite:4]{index=4}
Safety tip

Even when signed in with an institutionally managed account, avoid pasting highly sensitive data (detailed student records, confidential HR documents, unpublished research with IP constraints) directly into Gemini. Use it for drafts, scaffolding, and exploration.

2
Tour the Gemini browser interface

The web interface has evolved (Google has been iterating rapidly), but most recent versions share a similar layout: a central chat canvas and a sidebar for organization.:contentReference[oaicite:5]{index=5}

  • Main chat canvas: The large area where you type prompts and see responses. You’ll see starter suggestions (“Brainstorm ideas,” “Write a summary,” etc.) and a mode selector for different types of tasks.
  • Left sidebar: Recent chats, pinned conversations, and (in newer versions) folders like Chats, Gems, and My Stuff for files, uploads, and other artifacts.:contentReference[oaicite:6]{index=6}
  • Top bar: Model or plan indicator (e.g., Gemini free vs. Advanced), account info, and quick links to Google AI Studio or Workspace integrations.
Mental model

Treat gemini.google.com as your general-purpose thinking space: you can experiment freely with prompts, then move stable workflows into NotebookLM, Google Docs, or other tools once you know what works.

3
Warm up: Explanations, nuance, and self-critique

Before connecting Gemini to any of your files, explore how it explains concepts and handles nuance.

  1. In a new chat, ask for a core concept explanation:
    “Explain large language models to a first-year undergraduate in 3 short paragraphs.”
  2. Immediately ask for limitations:
    “List 4 limitations or risks of using large language models in grading or assessment.”
  3. Ask for different framings:
    “Now explain the same idea for faculty who are worried about academic integrity.”
  4. Push Gemini to critique itself:
    “What might be misleading, incomplete, or oversimplified in your explanation?”
Activity: Explanation comparison

Pick one topic from this certificate (e.g., hallucinations, prompt engineering, or retrieval-augmented generation). Ask Gemini to explain it, then:

  • Highlight where it’s strong and where it glosses over important details.
  • Write one short paragraph in your own words correcting or deepening the answer.
  • Ask Gemini to integrate your paragraph and compare the result.
4
Use Gemini for web-grounded research (with caution)

Gemini can perform multi-step web research, especially through features like Deep Research, to synthesize information from multiple sources.:contentReference[oaicite:7]{index=7}

  1. Ask a research-style question, such as:
    “What are emerging policies on generative AI use in higher education in the U.S.? Summarize patterns across at least 5 institutions.”
  2. If available in your region/account, enable or select Deep Research when prompted. Gemini will take longer and show that it is reading multiple sources.
  3. Once you get an answer, look for:
    • Embedded citations or links to external sources.
    • Specific claims you can verify independently.
    • Places where the answer is generic or vague.
  4. Follow up with:
    “For each claim, name the specific source and quote the relevant passage in your own words.”
Research stance

Use Gemini to narrow your reading list and surface candidate sources, not to replace primary reading. It’s a front-end research assistant, not a substitute for your own literature review.

5
Upload files and connect to Google Workspace

Gemini supports file uploads directly in the browser, and in many institutional setups it can also connect to your Google Workspace files (Drive, Docs, Gmail) if admins enable that connection.:contentReference[oaicite:8]{index=8}

  1. In Gemini’s chat, look for the Attach or Upload icon near the prompt box.
  2. Upload a single document that’s appropriate for cloud AI processing, such as:
    • a course syllabus or assignment prompt,
    • a policy or guidelines document,
    • a draft memo or report.
  3. Ask questions like:
    • “Summarize the key decisions and responsibilities in this document in 5 bullets.”
    • “What assumptions does this policy make about access, technology, or time?”
    • “Suggest 3 ways to rephrase this for students in clearer language.”
  4. If your Workspace admin has enabled it, you may see options to let Gemini access your Drive or Gmail directly. Turn these on only if you understand the implications and your institution approves.:contentReference[oaicite:9]{index=9}
Data boundaries

Gemini’s Workspace integrations can summarize Docs, Slides, and Sheets or help draft Gmail messages, but they respect your existing permissions. Use them for productivity, not for bypassing sharing rules or reviewing content you shouldn’t see.

6
Shape the conversation with roles, tasks, and iterations

Like other large language models, Gemini responds best when you specify role, task, audience, constraints, and iteration style.

  1. Start with a role and task:
    “You are an instructional designer at a public research university. Help me draft guidance for instructors on responsible generative AI use in take-home assignments.”
  2. Add audience and constraints:
    “Write 2 options under 250 words each, in accessible student-facing language, with one concrete example in each.”
  3. Ask Gemini to show its reasoning:
    “Explain why you structured each option this way and what tradeoffs you see.”
  4. Iterate:
    “Option 1 is closest to what I need. Shorten it by 30%, keep the example, and add one sentence on academic honesty.”
Activity: Reusable prompt pattern

Choose a recurring task (e.g., drafting announcements, designing assignment prompts, planning workshops). Build a prompt that includes:

  • Role
  • Audience
  • Constraints (length, tone, structure)
  • Request for reasoning and revision

Save this as a reusable pattern you can paste into Gemini or adapt in other tools.

7
Gems (custom agents) and Workspace tie-ins

In recent updates, Google has introduced Gems — customizable Gemini “personas” or agents that can be tailored for specific tasks (e.g., tutoring, copywriting, study help). These can appear inside the Gemini web app and, increasingly, in Workspace apps like Docs, Sheets, and Gmail.:contentReference[oaicite:10]{index=10}

  • Prebuilt Gems: Task-specific assistants (study helper, writing coach, data explainer) you can launch directly from the sidebar.
  • Custom Gems: Agents you or your institution define with instructions and example behaviors. In some setups, these can be surfaced inside Docs, Sheets, and Gmail side panels.
How to treat Gems

Think of Gems as pre-configured versions of Gemini. They may have extra context or specialized behaviors, but the core principle doesn’t change: you still need to review outputs, apply domain judgment, and respect data boundaries.

8
Wrap-up: Where Gemini fits in your AI toolkit

Gemini in the browser is strongest when you need:

  • Fast brainstorming and drafting across many topics
  • Web-grounded overviews and comparisons (with verification)
  • Lightweight file-based help for non-sensitive documents
  • A bridge into Google Workspace (Docs, Sheets, Slides, Gmail) when enabled

As you move between Gemini, NotebookLM, Copilot, and other tools in this certificate, keep asking: “Is this a fast generalist question for Gemini, or a deep, source-specific question for NotebookLM or another grounded tool?”

This page focuses on the browser-based Gemini experience at gemini.google.com. Google updates Gemini frequently, so buttons and layouts may shift, but the core workflow — chat, iterate, then connect to your documents when appropriate — remains the same.
