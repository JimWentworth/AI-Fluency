Tool Highlight: ChatGPT Free (with Projects & Group Chats)

Start with the free version, learn the core patterns, then decide if ChatGPT Edu is worth it.

In this walkthrough, you’ll see what you can do with ChatGPT Free today— including Projects and Group chats—and how it compares to the university-licensed ChatGPT Edu available through the Illinois WebStore.

What you get with ChatGPT Free

The Free plan of ChatGPT is designed for “intelligence for everyday tasks”: writing help, study support, planning, and quick questions.:contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}

Key capabilities on the free plan include:​:contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}

  • Real-time web search in chat for current topics.
  • Chat with files and images (upload documents or images and ask questions about them).
  • Study mode for step-by-step learning instead of just answers.:contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}
  • Limited image generation (slower and lower volume than paid plans).
  • Limited messages/uploads and memory/context per day.
  • Projects to organize your work (also available on paid plans).:contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}
  • Group chats so multiple people can collaborate with ChatGPT together.:contentReference[oaicite:4]{index=4}
Good to know

The free plan gives “limited access to reasoning” and tighter limits on messages, uploads, and images than paid plans—but the patterns you learn here transfer directly to Plus, Pro, or Edu later.:contentReference[oaicite:5]{index=5}

Sign in to ChatGPT Free and get oriented
  1. Go to https://chatgpt.com in a browser.:contentReference[oaicite:6]{index=6}
  2. Click Try ChatGPT Free or Log in.
  3. Create an account with email or a supported sign-in option.
  4. After logging in, notice:

    • Model picker: usually an “Auto” option that routes you to a suitable model for free usage.:contentReference[oaicite:7]{index=7}

Data hygiene

The public free plan is not campus-licensed. Avoid pasting confidential student data, unpublished research, or sensitive HR details. For that, ChatGPT Edu is the safer choice.:contentReference[oaicite:9]{index=9}

Using Projects on the free plan

Projects are “smart workspaces” that keep related chats, files, and instructions together so ChatGPT stays on-topic. They’re available to all plans, including Free.:contentReference[oaicite:10]{index=10}

  1. Click New project in the sidebar.
  2. Give it a name (for example, “AI Fluency Certificate Notes”).
  3. Optionally add an icon and color so it’s easy to spot.
  4. Add project instructions, such as:
    • “Assume I’m at the University of Illinois.”
    • “Be concise and use bullet points when summarizing research.”
    • “Ask clarifying questions before giving long answers.”
    These instructions apply only inside that project.:contentReference[oaicite:11]{index=11}
  5. Upload 1–2 files (e.g., syllabus PDF, assignment description) and start a chat inside the project.
Activity: Build a “Certificate Companion” project

Create a project called “AI Fluency Companion” and:

  • Paste in a short description of why you’re taking the certificate.
  • Upload one reading or assignment prompt from the course.
  • Ask ChatGPT to generate:
    • 3–5 key questions to keep in mind while you work through the program, and
    • a one-paragraph “learning contract” in your own words that you can revise.

This project can become your running workspace for the whole certificate, on free ChatGPT or later in ChatGPT Edu.

Free vs Edu

On the free plan, projects are personal. In Edu/Business/Enterprise, projects can be shared across a workspace, which is useful for departments or course teams.:contentReference[oaicite:12]{index=12}

Using Group chats (free feature)

Group chats let you bring other people—and ChatGPT—into the same conversation. They are available to logged-in users on Free, Go, Plus, and Pro plans.:contentReference[oaicite:13]{index=13}

  1. Start a new chat, then click the people icon (top-right) to create a group.
  2. Give the group a clear name, like “Module 2 Study Group”.
  3. Share the invite link with 1–20 classmates or colleagues.:contentReference[oaicite:14]{index=14}
  4. Decide how you’ll use ChatGPT in the group:
    • As a summarizer (“please summarize the last 20 messages”).
    • As a ref-finder (“what are two alternative perspectives on this idea?”).
    • As a facilitator (“suggest three next steps for our project”).
  5. When you explicitly want ChatGPT’s input, mention it by name in your message (e.g., “ChatGPT, can you draft a quick outline?”).:contentReference[oaicite:15]{index=15}
Activity: Mini design sprint in a group chat

With 2–4 people:

  • Create a group chat called “Responsible AI Practices at Illinois”.
  • Each person posts one concern about AI on campus.
  • Ask ChatGPT to:
    • Cluster the concerns into 3 themes, and
    • Suggest one practical experiment for each theme that could be piloted in a course or unit.
  • As humans, critique the suggestions. What’s realistic? What’s missing?
Privacy reminder

Group chats are separate from your individual conversations, and your personal memory isn’t shared—but everyone in the group can see what you post. Don’t treat it like a private 1:1.:contentReference[oaicite:16]{index=16}

4
Free ChatGPT vs ChatGPT Edu at Illinois

ChatGPT Free is a good “sandbox” for:

  • Learning prompts and workflows.
  • Low-stakes writing, planning, and study support.
  • Trying Projects and Group chats for coordination.

ChatGPT Edu is a paid, campus-licensed version for universities with:​:contentReference[oaicite:17]{index=17}

  • Expanded access to GPT-5 and higher message limits than free.
  • Advanced data analysis, web browsing, document summarization.
  • Custom GPTs and shared projects within a university workspace.
  • Stronger privacy and security controls; campus data not used to train models by default.

At Illinois, ChatGPT Edu licenses are available via the Illinois WebStore for faculty and staff (with student access planned later).:contentReference[oaicite:18]{index=18}
