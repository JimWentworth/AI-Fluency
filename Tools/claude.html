Tool Highlight: Claude (Free Version in the Browser)

Anthropic’s conversational AI at claude.ai, using the free tier.

In this walkthrough, you’ll go from creating a free Claude account to using the browser app for chat, drafting, and document work—while managing context and token limits so you don’t run out of room mid-conversation.

What Claude is (and why people like it)

Claude is Anthropic’s family of AI models, designed with an emphasis on helpfulness, honesty, and safety. The free browser version at claude.ai gives you access to a powerful general-purpose assistant that’s especially strong at:

  • Reading and reasoning over long documents
  • Structured writing, outlining, and editing
  • Complex reasoning and step-by-step problem solving

The free tier includes:

  • Access via web at claude.ai
  • A generous but finite number of messages and tokens per day (usage varies by time and model availability)
  • Occasional “You’ve reached your limit” messages when you’ve used a lot of context or many chats in a short time
Campus lens

In this certificate, we treat free Claude as a high-quality “lab bench” for exploring prompts and workflows. It’s great for experimentation—but because it’s a consumer-grade service, you should avoid using it for highly sensitive or regulated data.

1
Create or sign in to a free Claude account
  1. Open a browser and go to https://claude.ai.
  2. Click Sign up (for a new account) or Log in if you’ve used Claude before.
  3. You can typically sign up with:
    • an email + password, or
    • a Google or Apple account sign-in.
  4. Confirm your email if prompted. After that, you should land on the Claude workspace with a list of chats on the left and a large prompt box in the center.
Account choice

If your institution doesn’t provide an enterprise Claude subscription, treat your free claude.ai account as personal. Don’t upload confidential internal documents, granular student data, or anything covered by strict contracts or IRB.

2
Tour the Claude web interface

The Claude browser interface is minimal but powerful. Most recent versions share a layout like:

  • Left sidebar: Buttons for New chat, a list of your recent chats, and sometimes model or workspace controls (if available).
  • Main chat area: The center panel where you see conversation history, Claude’s responses, and any attached documents.
  • Prompt box: At the bottom: a large text box with icons for attaching files, images, or (in some versions) toggling features like “Projects” or tools.
Mental model

Think of each Claude chat as a self-contained working session. Claude remembers context within that chat up to its token limit, but does not “remember” across completely separate chats unless you copy information over yourself.

3
Warm up with explanations and self-critique

Start by seeing how Claude explains ideas and handles uncertainty, without any uploads yet.

  1. In a fresh chat, ask:
    “Explain retrieval-augmented generation for someone at a public research university who is new to AI.”
  2. Immediately follow up with:
    “List 4 limitations or risks of retrieval-augmented generation in higher education.”
  3. Ask for re-framing:
    “Now rewrite the explanation for a class of undergraduates in 2 short paragraphs.”
  4. Push Claude to critique itself:
    “What might be misleading, oversimplified, or incomplete in your explanation?”
Activity: Side-by-side comparison

Ask Claude, Copilot, and Gemini the same explanatory question. Compare:

  • Clarity and structure
  • Nuance and caveats
  • Where each one seems overconfident or vague

Decide where Claude’s style helps or hurts your understanding.

4
Upload documents (and watch the context size)

One of Claude’s strengths is handling long documents, but even “long context” has limits, especially on the free tier. If you paste or upload a huge amount of text, you can still run out of room in the conversation.

  1. Click the paperclip / file icon next to the prompt box.
  2. Upload one or two files such as:
    • a course syllabus or assignment prompt,
    • a policy document,
    • a draft report or memo.
  3. Ask Claude to work with them:
    • “Give me a 3-paragraph summary of this document.”
    • “What assumptions does this policy make about students or staff?”
    • “Suggest 3 improvements to make this more accessible and inclusive.”
  4. If the document is very long, start with narrower questions: “Focus only on the section about assessment and grading.”
Context discipline

The more pages and messages you stuff into a single chat, the more likely you are to hit context limits. In practice, it’s often smarter to:

  • Work with 1–3 key documents per chat, not 10–20.
  • Ask specific questions instead of “analyze everything in all files.”
  • Start a fresh chat when the conversation feels bloated or unfocused.
5
Understand token and usage limits (and how to avoid hitting them)

All large language models, including Claude, operate with a context window: a maximum number of “tokens” (chunks of text) they can consider at once. The free tier also adds daily usage limits.

  • When you paste long text, upload files, and have a long back-and-forth, you are filling up that context window.
  • When you send many prompts in a day, you are using up your free-tier quota.
  • When you hit limits, Claude may:
    • truncate earlier parts of the conversation,
    • refuse to continue until you start a new chat, or
    • show a “usage limit reached” or “come back later” message.
Strategies to stay under the limits

To avoid running out of tokens or daily messages in the middle of something important:

  • Keep each chat focused on one project or question.
  • Summarize: ask Claude to summarize long context into a short outline, then start a new chat using that outline instead of the full text.
  • Save stable instructions or prompts in a separate document and paste them when needed, rather than making one endless chat.
  • Export or copy important outputs before you get close to limits.
Certificate reminder

For this program, assume the free Claude tier is a shared resource: plan experiments so they fit in short, well-designed sessions, rather than leaving one massive chat open all day.

6
Shape Claude with roles, constraints, and iteration

Just like with other tools, Claude responds best when you specify role, audience, constraints, and how you want to iterate.

  1. Set the role and context:
    “You are an instructional designer at a public research university. I’m drafting guidance for instructors on how to use generative AI responsibly in coursework.”
  2. Specify audience and constraints:
    “Write 2 options under 250 words each in student-facing language, with at least one concrete example.”
  3. Ask for reasoning or critique:
    “Explain how you balanced clarity, nuance, and length. What tradeoffs did you make?”
  4. Iterate on the best draft:
    “Option 2 is closest. Shorten it by 25%, keep the example, and add one sentence about academic integrity.”
Activity: Prompt pattern you can reuse

Take a task you’ll do often (designing assignment prompts, drafting announcements, summarizing meetings) and create a Claude prompt that includes:

  • Role + audience
  • Output format and length
  • Constraints (tone, reading level, number of examples)
  • A request for critique or next steps

Save this pattern in a separate document so you can reuse it even if you hit usage limits and need to start a new chat.

7
Wrap-up: Where free Claude fits in your toolkit

Free Claude in the browser is strongest when you need:

  • Deep reasoning or structured writing on a single problem or document
  • Careful, step-by-step thinking about prompts and workflows
  • Experimentation with long-context behaviors (within reasonable limits)

Because the free tier has usage caps and context limits, it’s best for:

  • Short, well-scoped experiments
  • Designing prompt patterns you can reuse across tools
  • Developing drafts you’ll later refine in other systems (Copilot, Docs, Word, etc.)

As you work through this certificate, ask: “Is this task a good fit for a focused Claude session, or do I need a tool that’s more tightly integrated with campus systems or specific data sources?”

This page focuses on the free browser version of Claude at claude.ai. Anthropic updates limits, models, and UI over time, so exact quotas and buttons may shift, but the core practices—scoping context, managing tokens, and exporting important work—remain the same.
