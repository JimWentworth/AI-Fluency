Tool Highlight: Google NotebookLM

A research assistant that works directly on the documents, links, and media you upload.

In this activity, you’ll take NotebookLM from “never opened it” to using the Studio tools to generate overviews, study aids, and presentation-ready artifacts from your own sources.

Best for: Reading & research Great with long PDFs & articles Grounded in your uploaded sources
0
Before you start: Safety & accounts

NotebookLM is a Google tool that analyzes only the sources you load into each notebook. It’s powerful for course readings, reports, policies, and research notes—but you still need to make good choices about what you upload.

  • Only upload materials you are allowed to share under campus policies.
  • Avoid highly sensitive data (e.g., confidential HR files, detailed student records).
  • For class work, start with readings, syllabi, slides, datasets, or your own writing.
Tip

Each notebook is siloed: NotebookLM only sees sources inside that notebook, not across all your Google Drive. This makes it well-suited for course- or project-specific research.

1
Sign in to NotebookLM
  1. Open a browser and go to notebooklm.google.com.
  2. Click Sign in and choose the Google account you’ll use for your work (for course or campus work, use your Illinois-associated Google account if available).
  3. Accept any terms or “Labs/experimental” notices. NotebookLM is still evolving, and features may change during the year.
If you don’t see it

Some institutional Google Workspace configurations may limit access. If you can’t open NotebookLM with your campus account, try a personal Google account with non-sensitive materials only, or check with your local IT contact.

2
Create a notebook for your project

A notebook is a container for one project or topic. All your sources and AI-generated artifacts for that project live here.

  1. On the left side, click New notebook (or the + tile).
  2. Give it a clear name, such as “AI Ethics Syllabus – Fall 2025” or “Grant Proposal Background Reading.”
  3. Click Create. You’ll see an empty notebook with three main regions: Sources, Chat, and Studio.
Concept check

NotebookLM doesn’t search the open web by default. It works primarily from what you load into the notebook. If something isn’t in your sources, the model may decline to answer or tell you it doesn’t have enough information.

3
Add sources (your readings, files, and links)

Now give NotebookLM something to work with. Start with 1–5 items so it’s manageable.

  1. In the Sources panel, click Add source (or the + button).
  2. Choose from options such as:
    • PDFs, text, or markdown files from your computer
    • Google Docs or Slides (import from Drive)
    • Web URLs (articles, documentation, reports)
    • YouTube or audio files for lecture-style content
    • Copied & pasted text (for shorter snippets or notes)
  3. Wait while NotebookLM processes each source. You’ll see them listed in the Sources panel.
  4. Click a source name to preview or skim it inside NotebookLM, or open the original in a new tab if you prefer.
Good starting set

For this certificate, a nice starter set is: one reading (PDF or article), one syllabus or assignment prompt, and one of your own documents (e.g., notes or a draft).

4
Understand the three-panel layout

Most of your work happens in three panels. It’s worth pausing to understand what each one is for before you start clicking buttons.

  • Sources (left): All the files, links, and notes you’ve added to this notebook.
  • Chat (center): A conversation window where you ask questions about your sources, get summaries, and request transformations.
  • Studio (right): A workspace for creating richer artifacts (audio/video overviews, mind maps, study guides, infographics, slide decks, quizzes, flashcards, etc.) from your sources.
Mental model

Think of Chat as “quick questions and drafts” and Studio as “things I might actually keep, share, or present.”

5
Chat with your sources

Before you touch the Studio tools, it’s useful to see how NotebookLM answers basic questions grounded in your documents.

  1. Click into the Chat panel. NotebookLM will often show a Notebook Guide with a summary and suggested questions.
  2. Try a simple prompt such as:
    • “Give me a 3-paragraph overview of the key arguments across these sources.”
    • “What are three points of disagreement between Source A and Source B?”
    • “Explain the main idea in language a first-year student could understand.”
  3. Skim the answer and scroll down to see which sources and citations it references.
  4. Ask a follow-up that pushes on reasoning or gaps, for example: “What’s missing from this summary that might matter for policy decisions?”
Use it critically

Even though NotebookLM is grounded in your sources, it can still misread nuance or over- simplify. Treat its responses as drafts or hypotheses to check against the originals, not as authoritative answers.

6
Use the Studio tools (Audio, Video, Maps, Infographics, Slides…)

Now we move into the Studio panel, where NotebookLM turns your sources into more structured learning and presentation artifacts. Each tool is one click to generate, with optional customization panels.

6A. Core Studio tools overview

Audio Overview Listen

Generates a podcast-style conversation between AI “hosts” that walks through the key ideas in your sources. Useful for review while commuting or doing other work.

Video Overview Watch

Creates a narrated slideshow with images, quotes, and key points pulled from your documents—like a visual counterpart to the audio overview.

Mind Map & Reports Structure

Generates concept maps, study guides, FAQs, timelines, and briefing docs to organize complex material into digestible chunks.

Infographic & Slide Deck Present

Turns your sources into visual summaries and slide decks, with options to tweak length, language, and emphasis.

Flashcards & Quizzes Practice

Builds question sets from your notebook that you can use for self-testing or share with students for formative review.

Learning Guide Tutor

Walks you through a topic step-by-step with explanations and follow-up questions, like a structured study session based on your sources.

6B. Step-by-step: Generate an Audio Overview

  1. In your notebook, open the Studio panel.
  2. Click the Audio Overview tile.
  3. Optionally, open the customization panel (pencil icon) and set preferences such as: desired length, tone (e.g., “conversational for undergraduates”), or specific sections to emphasize.
  4. Click Generate. NotebookLM will create the audio in the background.
  5. When it’s ready, press Play to listen. You can adjust playback speed and pause/rewind as needed.
  6. When you hear something important, pause and add your own note in the notebook—or in a separate document—rather than treating the audio as the final word.
Try this as a learning activity

Choose one dense reading, generate an Audio Overview, and then compare:

  • What the audio emphasized vs. what you think matters most.
  • Any missing perspectives, limitations, or counter-arguments.
  • How you might revise the customization prompt to get a better overview next time.

6C. Step-by-step: Create a Video Overview

  1. In the Studio panel, select Video Overview.
  2. (Optional) Customize the focus: for example, ask it to visualize “workflow diagrams” or “key concepts and their relationships” rather than everything.
  3. Click Generate and wait for the narrated slideshow to be built.
  4. Play the video, noting where visuals help—or oversimplify—the underlying material.
  5. Decide whether any slide is good enough to copy into your own teaching materials or presentation (with edits and proper attribution).

6D. Step-by-step: Mind Maps & Reports

  1. In Studio, choose Mind Map or a type of report (Study Guide, FAQ, Briefing Doc, Timeline, etc.).
  2. Optionally add a short instruction such as: “Organize concepts around ethical, technical, and policy themes.”
  3. Click Generate and review the output:
  • Does the structure match how you’d organize the topic?
  • What sections should be merged, split, or renamed?
  • Where would you add human commentary, examples, or campus-specific context?

6E. Step-by-step: Build an Infographic or Slide Deck

  1. In Studio, select Infographic or Slide Deck.
  2. Open the customization panel and set:
    • Audience (e.g., “first-year undergraduates in CS”)
    • Length (short / default / long)
    • Focus (e.g., “key definitions and 3 examples”)
  3. Click Generate and wait for the artifact to appear in the Studio list.
  4. Open it and scan for:
    • Factual accuracy and alignment with your sources
    • Any misleading graphics, metaphors, or missing caveats
    • Slides or panels that are strong enough to reuse with edits
  5. Download the infographic (usually as an image file) or slide deck (often as a PDF) and annotate or revise it in your preferred design or slide tool.

6F. Step-by-step: Flashcards, Quizzes, and Learning Guide

  1. In Studio, choose Flashcards or Quiz.
  2. Narrow the topic if needed (e.g., “Only cover the sections on retrieval-augmented generation”).
  3. Generate the set, then quickly scan for quality:
  • Are the questions aligned with what you actually want learners to remember?
  • Are the answers accurate and appropriately scoped?
  1. Use or share the best questions. Edit or discard any that are confusing, trivial, or off-target.
  2. For deeper study, open the Learning Guide tool and follow the step-by-step prompts to work through a concept using your own sources.
For this certificate

A good mini-project is to pick one topic from the program, load 3–5 sources into a notebook, and then:

  • Generate an Audio or Video Overview,
  • Create either a Study Guide or Infographic, and
  • Export at least one artifact you’d actually use or refine further.

Your job is not to admire what NotebookLM makes—it’s to critique, adapt, and document where human judgment is still required.

7
Wrap-up: How this fits into AI fluency

NotebookLM is one example of a “source-grounded” AI tool: instead of chatting with a general-purpose model about everything, you constrain it to the texts, media, and notes you care about. In this certificate, that makes it a useful companion for:

  • Reading dense or technical material and surfacing key ideas
  • Comparing multiple documents (syllabi, policies, articles) on one theme
  • Designing teaching artifacts and study aids from the same underlying sources
  • Exploring how far you can push “automation” before human expertise is clearly needed

As you continue, ask yourself: What parts of this workflow can I safely delegate to NotebookLM, and where do I need to slow down and think for myself?

You can revisit this page any time you want a structured walkthrough. Features in NotebookLM evolve frequently, so specific buttons or labels may shift—but the overall workflow (Sources → Chat → Studio) remains a solid mental model.
