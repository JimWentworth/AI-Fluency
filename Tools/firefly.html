Tool Highlight: Adobe Firefly in the Browser

A web-based creative space for generating and editing images with generative AI.

In this walkthrough, you’ll go from logging in at firefly.adobe.com to using core browser tools: Text to Image, Generative Fill, and Generative Expand to extend your canvas.

What Firefly is (and why it matters)

Adobe Firefly is a suite of generative AI tools available directly in the browser at firefly.adobe.com. It can generate and edit images, video, and audio using natural language prompts and intuitive controls. It’s designed as a creative co-pilot, not a replacement for human judgment.

  • Web app first: You can use Firefly entirely in the browser without installing Photoshop or other desktop apps.:contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}
  • Commercially safe (per Adobe): Firefly models are trained on licensed and permissioned content (Adobe Stock, open-licensed, public domain), and are marketed as safe for commercial use, especially on qualifying plans.:contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}
  • Credit-based system: Free accounts get a limited number of generative credits per month; paid plans include more.:contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}
Campus lens

Firefly is helpful for mockups, study visuals, and teaching materials. You still need to follow campus and copyright policies when using generated images in publications, marketing, or course materials.

1
Sign in at firefly.adobe.com
  1. Open a browser and go to https://firefly.adobe.com. :contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}
  2. Click Sign in. Use either:
    • Your Illinois-linked Adobe account (if provided by campus), or
    • A personal Adobe ID for non-sensitive, non-institutional work.
  3. Complete any two-factor steps. Once logged in, you’ll see the Firefly home page with tiles like Text to Image, Generative Fill, and AI Photo Editor.
  4. If you use other Adobe tools (Photoshop, Express), the same account helps Firefly sync content across apps via Creative Cloud.:contentReference[oaicite:4]{index=4}
Safety tip

For graded student work, HR content, or other sensitive materials, avoid uploading anything you wouldn’t be comfortable storing in a third-party cloud service, even with Adobe’s protections.

The exact layout may change as Adobe updates Firefly, but the browser app usually centers on three concepts: generation tools, editing tools, and (in newer versions) Boards.

  • Home tiles: Launchers for tools like Text to Image, Text Effects, Generative Fill, and video/audio generators.:contentReference[oaicite:5]{index=5}

Mental model

Think of Firefly as three layers: ideation (Text to Image, Boards), editing (Generative Fill, Extend Canvas), and production tweaks (background removal, resizing, quick effects).

3
Start with Text to Image

Text to Image is the quickest way to get something visual from a concept, course topic, or workshop theme.

  1. On the Firefly home page, click Text to Image.:contentReference[oaicite:8]{index=8}
  2. In the prompt box, describe what you want, including:
    • Subject: “a student studying in the library”
    • Style: “flat illustration, limited color palette”
    • Use: “for a syllabus header”
    Example: “Flat illustration of a diverse group of students studying together at a table in a bright library, simple shapes and limited Illinois color palette, for a syllabus header.”
  3. Adjust Style, Lighting, Color, or Composition in the settings pane and click Generate.:contentReference[oaicite:9]{index=9}
  4. Review the four variations. Use:
    • Refresh to regenerate variations
    • Style presets to quickly switch aesthetics (photo, watercolor, etc.)
    • Download when you have a useful draft
Activity: Concept-to-visual exercise

Pick one abstract topic from your discipline (e.g., “algorithmic bias,” “peer review,” “student help-seeking”). Create:

  • One serious, informational image
  • One playful or metaphorical image

Reflect: Which is more effective for your audience? Where could each go wrong?

4
Use Generative Fill to add, remove, or replace

Generative Fill lets you upload a photo or Firefly-generated image and then add, remove, or replace content using brushes and prompts.:contentReference[oaicite:10]{index=10}

  1. From the Firefly home page, click Generative Fill.
  2. Upload an image (e.g., a campus scene, a workshop photo) or choose a sample asset.
  3. Use the selection brush to highlight an area:
    • Remove objects: Brush over a distracting sign or person and click Generate with no prompt.
    • Add objects: Select an area and prompt, e.g., “add a small potted plant on the table.”
    • Replace background: Select background and prompt, “soft gradient background in Illinois colors.”:contentReference[oaicite:11]{index=11}
  4. Scroll through variations, pick the best one, and export when you’re satisfied—or roll back changes if something feels off.
Ethics check

Avoid altering images of real people in ways that misrepresent events or individuals (e.g., adding people to meetings they never attended). For instruction, clearly label edited images as demonstrations.

5
Extend your canvas with Generative Expand

Sometimes you have a good image but not the right aspect ratio. Firefly’s Generative Expand (extend canvas) lets you enlarge the image and fill in the new space with coherent content.:contentReference[oaicite:12]{index=12}

  1. Either:
    • Start from Generative Fill and open an image, or
    • Use a Board or image-editing view where you see an Extend or Generative Expand option on the canvas.
  2. Choose Expand / Extend canvas. Drag the handles to widen or heighten your image (for example, from 4:3 to 16:9 to fit a slide or video frame).
  3. Option A: Leave the prompt blank and let Firefly auto-complete the edges based on the existing image.
    Option B: Add a prompt to control the extension, e.g., “extend the sky and city skyline, soft sunset colors, no new people.”
  4. Click Generate. Review the expanded variations:
    • Check for obvious seams or artifacts at the boundaries.
    • Zoom in on text, faces, or important objects; regenerate if they look distorted.
  5. When satisfied, download the extended image for use in slides, banners, or web headers.
Activity: Slide/header prep

Take an existing image you use in teaching, a syllabus, or a website:

  • Use Generative Expand to create a wider version suitable for a slide or banner.
  • Leave one side relatively simple so you have space for text.
  • Compare the original and extended versions for visual continuity and clarity.
6
Experiment with Text Effects (optional but fun)

Text Effects lets you generate stylized lettering where the letters are “filled” with patterns or imagery based on your prompt—for example, “AI” made of circuit boards or “Welcome” made of books.:contentReference[oaicite:13]{index=13}

  1. On the Firefly home page, choose Text Effects (name may vary slightly).
  2. Type your text, e.g., “AI Fluency”.
  3. In the prompt box, describe the effect, such as: “letters made out of books and notebooks, simple, clean, minimal background.”
  4. Adjust style settings (3D vs flat, photography vs illustration) and click Generate.
  5. Export a version that would work as a title slide or section divider in your course.
Use with intention

It’s easy to overdo visual effects. Aim for readability and accessibility: avoid low-contrast combinations and overly busy textures behind small text.

7
Wrap-up: Where Firefly fits in your AI toolkit

Firefly in the browser is strongest when you need visual help: concept art, teaching graphics, mockups, and image cleanup. Its core strengths are:

  • Quick image generation from text for slides, handouts, and web content
  • Background removal and object editing with Generative Fill
  • Aspect-ratio fixes and extended canvases using Generative Expand

As you use Firefly alongside tools like Copilot, NotebookLM, or ChatGPT, keep asking: Which parts of this visual workflow can I safely automate—and where do I need to slow down and make explicit design, ethics, or accessibility decisions myself?

This page reflects the browser-based Firefly experience at firefly.adobe.com. Adobe updates Firefly frequently, so tool names and layouts may evolve, but the basic workflow — generate, edit, extend — remains the same.
