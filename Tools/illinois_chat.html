What Illinois Chat is (and why it exists)

Illinois Chat is an official, campus-developed AI platform from NCSA’s Center for AI Innovation, built in partnership with the Office of the CIO and Illinois Computes. It gives the entire campus community access to large language model (LLM) capabilities through a secure, locally governed system.:contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}

Instead of just “one chatbot,” Illinois Chat is a platform where you can:

  • Create personalized AI assistants (chatbots) for courses, units, research, or projects.
  • Ground them in your own data: documents, websites, and other local sources.:contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}
  • Control the chatbot’s tone, audience, and capabilities.
  • Share them with others like you would a Google Doc (link-based access).:contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}
Eligibility & data classification

Eligibility: Faculty, staff, and students. Data: Approved for public and internal data only—not high-risk data.:contentReference[oaicite:4]{index=4}

How to access Illinois Chat
  1. Go to the Illinois Chat website: chat.illinois.edu.:contentReference[oaicite:5]{index=5}
  2. Click Sign in and log in with your University of Illinois credentials (NetID and password) via campus single sign-on.
  3. After login, you’ll see a dashboard listing:
    • Any chatbots you’ve created or have access to.
    • Options to create a new chatbot or explore examples (e.g., CropWizard).:contentReference[oaicite:6]{index=6}
When to reach for Illinois Chat

Use Illinois Chat when you need a repeatable assistant (e.g., a course bot or research helper) grounded in specific sources and safe for campus data, rather than a one-off conversation like in consumer ChatGPT.:contentReference[oaicite:7]{index=7}

2
How Illinois Chat works (at a high level)

Under the hood, Illinois Chat is built on:

  • Open-source LLMs tuned for academic and research environments.:contentReference[oaicite:8]{index=8}
  • A Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) framework: it first retrieves relevant snippets from your sources, then uses an LLM to generate an answer grounded in those snippets.:contentReference[oaicite:9]{index=9}
  • Support for document uploads, website scraping, and Canvas integration in some deployments and examples.:contentReference[oaicite:10]{index=10}

This architecture makes it well-suited for:

  • Course-specific teaching assistants.
  • Domain-focused research assistants.
  • Unit-level Q&A bots that answer from policy/FAQ documents and campus websites.
Key difference from “just ChatGPT”

Instead of talking to a general model trained on the public internet, Illinois Chat emphasizes chatbots that you configure, using sources you choose, in a campus-hosted environment.:contentReference[oaicite:11]{index=11}

3
Create your first Illinois Chat bot
  1. From the dashboard, click New chatbot (or similar “Create” option).
  2. Give your bot a descriptive name, such as:
    • “AI Fluency FAQ Assistant”
    • “Graduate Advising Info Bot”
    • “Research Group Helper – Lab Policies”
  3. Set the bot description in plain language:
    • Example: “You are a helpful assistant for the AI Fluency Certificate at the University of Illinois. Answer questions using only the course syllabus, module descriptions, and official program documentation. If you’re not sure, say you’re not sure and suggest who to contact.”
  4. Choose your main data source type to start:
    • Upload documents (PDF, DOCX, etc.).
    • Add URLs to scrape relevant campus sites.:contentReference[oaicite:12]{index=12}
  5. Save the bot and open the chat interface to try a few test questions.
Activity: Build a “Certificate FAQ” bot

Using Illinois Chat:

  • Create a bot named “AI Fluency Certificate FAQ”.
  • Upload the certificate syllabus + key policy documents.
  • Give it a centered instruction like: “Only answer based on these documents; if something isn’t covered, say so.”
  • Test:
    • “What are the core modules?”
    • “How long does the final project take?”
    • “Who should I contact for technical issues?”

Note where the bot does well, where it’s vague, and where you might need to adjust the data or instructions.

4
Strengthen your bot: better data, better answers

Illinois Chat gets its strength from the sources you connect. Examples from campus use include:

  • Course bots trained on syllabi, lecture notes, FAQs, and assignment descriptions.:contentReference[oaicite:13]{index=13}
  • Research bots like CropWizard, which consult over 200,000 curated agricultural publications using Illinois Chat’s RAG framework.:contentReference[oaicite:14]{index=14}
  • Unit-level bots that scrape campus websites to surface key resources and policies.:contentReference[oaicite:15]{index=15}

For your first bot, consider:

  • Course: upload syllabus + key how-to documents.
  • Advising: upload checklists, FAQs, and policy summaries.
  • Research: upload a curated folder of articles or internal docs (public/internal only).
Caution on sources

Don’t feed Illinois Chat high-risk data (e.g., protected health information, highly sensitive HR records) and avoid proprietary third-party datasets you’re not allowed to process with AI. Stick to public and internal data as defined by campus data classification guidelines.:contentReference[oaicite:16]{index=16}

Example use cases on campus

Illinois Chat is already being used across campus for teaching, research, and outreach:

  • Course assistants: course-specific bots in CS and engineering classes helping students with concept review and assignment-level questions.:contentReference[oaicite:17]{index=17}
  • Research helpers: bots like CropWizard that answer domain-specific questions, consult large curated corpora, and integrate with analytical tools.:contentReference[oaicite:18]{index=18}
  • Campus information bots: bots that scrape Illinois sites to help users discover resources, policies, and services more quickly.:contentReference[oaicite:19]{index=19}
Activity: “What kind of bot would help my unit?”

Take 5 minutes to sketch answers (on paper or in a doc):

  • Who is the audience? (students, staff, faculty, external partners?)
  • What top 10–20 questions do they ask repeatedly?
  • What existing documents/web pages already answer those questions?
  • What is off-limits (sensitive data, nuanced judgment, policy decisions)?

Use this sketch as a design brief when you create your first Illinois Chat bot.
