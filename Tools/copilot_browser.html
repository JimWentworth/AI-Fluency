Tool Highlight: Copilot Chat in the Browser

Secure, web-based AI chat for brainstorming, drafting, and exploring ideas.

In this walkthrough, you’ll go from logging in with your Illinois account, to running effective chats in the browser-based Copilot experience, with a brief note on extensions and Copilot agents.

Before you start: Which Copilot are we using?

Microsoft now has multiple Copilot experiences. In this page, we focus on Copilot Chat in the browser — the web-based interface you access at copilot.microsoft.com or via an enterprise Copilot Chat URL.

  • In this certificate, you should sign in with your @illinois.edu account so you benefit from campus protections and administrative controls.
  • Copilot Chat in the browser is primarily grounded in web data, with the ability to work on files you upload or content you paste into the conversation.:contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}
  • For deeper integration with your Illinois files (OneDrive, SharePoint, Outlook), you’ll typically use Copilot inside Microsoft 365 apps — covered elsewhere.
About the browser extensions

You can install Copilot-related extensions from the Edge Add-ons store or the Chrome Web Store to bring Copilot into a browser sidebar while you browse.:contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1} Those are useful extras, but this page focuses on the main browser chat experience itself.

Log in to Copilot Chat with your Illinois account
  1. Open a browser and go to https://copilot.microsoft.com. :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}
  2. Click Sign in.
  3. Enter your NetID@illinois.edu email and continue.
  4. You’ll be redirected to the Illinois single sign-on page — log in with your NetID and password, plus MFA if prompted.
  5. After login, look for:
    • Your Illinois identity in the top-right profile area.
    • In some enterprise Copilot Chat experiences, a shield or notice indicating that your data is protected and governed by organizational policies.:contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}
Check your account

If you see a personal Microsoft account or a warning that data might be used to improve services, you’re probably not in the campus-protected environment. Sign out and back in with your @illinois.edu identity before using institutional content.

2
Tour the Copilot Chat interface

Once signed in, you’ll see a layout that may look slightly different depending on the exact Copilot Chat release, but most versions share three main areas.:contentReference[oaicite:4]{index=4}

  • Left sidebar: Recent chats, pinned conversations, and sometimes agents or specialized Copilot experiences you can select.
  • Main chat panel: The large center area where you enter prompts, see responses, and view suggested starter prompts.
  • Toolbar / controls near the prompt box:
    • Attachment icon to add files or images.
    • Controls for mode (e.g., more precise vs. more creative) and whether Copilot can use the web for this conversation.
    • Voice / read-aloud options in some builds, for accessibility.:contentReference[oaicite:5]{index=5}
Mental model

Think of Copilot Chat as your general-purpose conversation space: you can reason through ideas, ask for drafts, test scenarios, and review documents, but every answer still needs checking, especially for high-stakes decisions.

3
Warm up with simple, critical chat prompts

Start by exploring how Copilot explains ideas and handles uncertainty, before you hand it important work.

  1. In a fresh chat, ask for an explanation:
    “Explain retrieval-augmented generation in 3 short paragraphs for a non-technical audience.”
  2. Follow up immediately with:
    “List 4 limitations or failure modes of retrieval-augmented generation, and where it shouldn’t be used.”
  3. Ask for alternative framings:
    “Give two different analogies to explain this concept to students in different majors.”
  4. Use Copilot as a skeptic:
    “If I presented your explanation in a faculty workshop, what pushback might I get?”
Activity: Explanation audit

Choose one concept from this certificate (e.g., large language models, hallucinations, or prompt engineering). Ask Copilot to explain it, then:

  • Mark anything misleading, overly confident, or missing important caveats.
  • Write a one-sentence “fix” you would add as a human expert or informed learner.
  • Ask Copilot to incorporate your fix and compare the new version.
4
Use the web grounding critically

Copilot Chat is typically grounded in web data. This is helpful for finding and synthesizing information quickly, but it also means you’re downstream of whatever is on the public web, plus model limitations.:contentReference[oaicite:6]{index=6}

  1. Ask a current-events or “what is” question you can easily verify, such as:
    “Summarize three recent changes in generative AI regulation in higher education.”
  2. Scroll through Copilot’s answer and look for:
    • Citations or links it provides to external sources.
    • Statements that feel too vague or generic.
    • Claims you can quickly fact-check with a trusted source.
  3. Ask a follow-up that forces Copilot to show its work:
    “For each claim, tell me which specific source you relied on and what that source actually says.”
Practice skepticism

When Copilot pulls from the web, treat it as an assistant summarizing search results, not as an oracle. Use it to narrow your reading, not to bypass it.

5
Upload files and chat about your own content

One of the most useful browser features is chatting with your own documents without leaving the Copilot site.:contentReference[oaicite:7]{index=7}

  1. Click the attachment icon near the prompt box.
  2. Upload a file that is appropriate for the campus environment: for example,
    • a syllabus or assignment prompt,
    • a policy document,
    • a draft memo or report that doesn’t contain sensitive data.
  3. Once the upload completes, try prompts such as:
    • “Summarize the key decisions this document describes in 5 bullet points.”
    • “What assumptions does this policy make about students’ access to technology?”
    • “Propose two alternative framings that might be more inclusive.”
  4. Ask Copilot to argue with itself: “Now critique your own summary. What might be misleading or incomplete?”
Data boundaries

Even with enterprise protections, avoid uploading highly sensitive documents (e.g., detailed personnel files, granular student records). Use Copilot for drafts, structure, and critique, not for final storage of confidential data.

6
Shape the conversation: roles, constraints, and iterations

Copilot Chat improves dramatically when you specify role, audience, constraints, and iteration style, rather than giving short, vague prompts.

  1. Start with a role and audience:
    “You are an instructional designer helping faculty at a public research university. I’m drafting guidance on responsible AI use in coursework.”
  2. Add constraints:
    “Generate 3 options under 200 words each, written in accessible, student-facing language.”
  3. Ask for reasoning instead of just output:
    “Explain why you chose these three approaches and what tradeoffs you see.”
  4. Use iterative refinement:
    “Option 2 is closest to what I need. Revise it to be shorter, and add one concrete example for STEM and one for humanities.”
Activity: Prompt tuning loop

Pick a real task you care about (designing a survey, explaining a policy, planning a workshop). Run three rounds of:

  1. Initial prompt with role + audience + constraints
  2. Ask Copilot to critique its own output (“what’s weak or missing?”)
  3. Revise your prompt based on that critique and your own judgment

Capture your “final” prompt as a reusable pattern for future work.

7
Aside: Copilot agents and custom copilots

In some versions of Copilot Chat, you’ll see specialized agents or GPTs in the left panel — for example, data analysis helpers, policy assistants, or institution-specific bots. These are:

  • Prebuilt agents from Microsoft or partners, designed for narrow tasks (like learning guides, coding helpers, or documentation search).:contentReference[oaicite:8]{index=8}
  • Custom copilots your organization creates using tools like Copilot Studio or GPT builders, connected to specific internal data sources.:contentReference[oaicite:9]{index=9}
How to treat agents

For this certificate, think of agents as pre-configured chat partners: they may have extra context or capabilities, but the same rules apply. You still need to:

  • Check their outputs for accuracy and bias.
  • Respect data boundaries when you share content.
  • Decide where their suggestions end and your judgment begins.
8
Wrap-up: Copilot Chat as a thinking partner

Copilot in the browser is best understood as a thinking partner with web access: it can generate, summarize, reframe, and probe ideas, but it can’t take responsibility for decisions, context, or ethics.

  • Use it to explore options, not to rubber-stamp answers.
  • Combine web-grounded chat with your own readings and domain knowledge.
  • Capture good prompt patterns so you can reuse them later.

As you move through the rest of the certificate, notice when Copilot helps you expand your thinking — and when you need to deliberately step away and reason without it.
