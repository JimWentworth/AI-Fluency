Hands-On Introduction to Generative AI

Notion

Notion is a flexible workspace for notes, documents, and databases. In teaching and learning contexts, it is especially well suited for reflective journaling, metacognition, and process documentation.

Notion is not an AI tutor or grading tool. Its value comes from supporting thinking over time, not generating answers.

Best uses in teaching and learning

  • Reflective journals and learning logs
  • Process documentation for projects or research
  • Metacognitive check-ins (“What did I try? What changed?”)
  • Portfolio-style collections of work
  • Planning and tracking long-term assignments

How Notion supports reflection

Notion allows reflections to accumulate across time while remaining organized. Students can revisit earlier thinking, identify growth, and connect feedback to future decisions.

Common structures:
  • One page per reflection entry
  • A database with dated entries
  • Tagged reflections by assignment or concept
  • Embedded images, links, or drafts

AI features and context considerations

Notion includes optional AI features that can assist with rewriting, summarizing, or questioning text. These tools operate on the local page or selection, not across an entire workspace unless explicitly prompted.

  • AI does not automatically “know” course context
  • Outputs are not grounded in external academic sources
  • Generated text should always be reviewed and revised
Key point: Notion’s AI features are best used to support reflection, not replace it.

Recommended reflection prompts

  • What was the most confusing or challenging part of this assignment?
  • What assumptions did I make at the start?
  • What feedback changed my thinking?
  • Where did I revise my approach, and why?
  • How did tools (including AI, if used) shape my process?

Academic integrity considerations

Reflective journals are intended to surface a student’s own thinking. Using AI to generate reflections undermines this purpose.

Appropriate uses:
  • Asking follow-up questions after writing
  • Clarifying language or organization
  • Identifying themes across multiple entries
Inappropriate uses:
  • Generating reflective responses from scratch
  • Submitting AI-written reflections as personal analysis
  • Using AI to mask lack of engagement

If AI meaningfully shapes reflective writing, students should follow course policies for acknowledgment and disclosure.

Faculty guidance

Reflection is best assessed for engagement and growth, not writing polish. Many instructors grade reflections for completion or use a lightweight rubric.

Assessment signals to look for:
  • Specific references to course experiences
  • Evidence of revision or changed understanding
  • Willingness to identify uncertainty
  • Connections between feedback and next steps

Limitations

  • Not a learning management system
  • Not designed for automated assessment
  • AI features are not source-grounded
  • Requires clear guidance on sharing and privacy

Bottom line

Notion is most powerful when used to document learning as a process. It supports metacognition, reflection, and academic integrity—not by policing, but by making thinking visible.
