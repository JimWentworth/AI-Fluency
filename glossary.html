AI Fluency Certificate Glossary

University of Illinois · AI Fluency

Use this glossary to clarify key concepts and decision-points that appear across the AI Fluency certificate. Select a term to expand its definition and see where it primarily shows up in the curriculum.

Agent / Custom Agent / GPT ▶
A configured AI workflow designed to perform a recurring task using stored instructions, files, or retrieval. Agents are most useful when you want predictable, repeatable outputs instead of one-off prompts.
Module 3 – Customization
Bias (Model Bias) ▶
Systematic distortions in an AI model’s outputs caused by patterns in its training data. Bias is a statistical artifact, not a moral intention, but it still carries real-world consequences that users must anticipate and manage.
Module 1 – Understanding GenAI
Branching a Chat ▶
Starting a new conversation from an earlier point so you can explore a different direction without overwriting or losing the original thread. Branching helps avoid cross-contamination of context when you want to test alternate approaches.
Module 2 – Iterative Collaboration
Context Window ▶
The maximum amount of input (measured in tokens) that the model can “see” at once. Larger context windows allow you to include longer documents, multiple examples, or extended instructions in a single interaction.
Module 1 – Understanding GenAI
Creative Steering ▶
Using prompts not merely to correct the model, but to actively shape direction: adjusting framing, constraints, and perspectives to guide idea exploration.
Module 2 – Iterative Collaboration
Custom Instructions ▶
Persistent settings that tell an AI system how to respond across conversations: your role, goals, audiences, tone, or constraints. They’re powerful for consistency but must be managed carefully to avoid unintended influence or privacy risks.
Module 3 – Customization
Data Protection Agreement / Campus-Safe AI ▶
Institutional agreements and tools (such as Illinois Chat) that ensure AI use complies with privacy, security, and regulatory requirements (e.g., FERPA, HIPAA, GDPR). These distinguish sensitive internal work from casual, commercial use.
Module 3 – Customization
Diffusion Model ▶
A generative model for images, audio, or video that learns to reverse noise. During generation, it starts from random static and iteratively “denoises” toward an output guided by a prompt.
Module 4 – Multimodal GenAI
Divergent Thinking (with AI) ▶
Using AI to expand the space of possibilities—generating multiple options, framings, or hypotheses before narrowing down. Especially useful in low-risk, exploratory phases of work.
Module 2 – Iterative Collaboration
Generative AI ▶
A class of models that create new content—text, images, audio, video— by predicting likely patterns based on training data. They simulate understanding by pattern prediction rather than true comprehension.
Module 1 – Understanding GenAI
Hallucination ▶
A confident but incorrect output generated by the model. Hallucinations are especially likely when the model is asked for specific facts that are not in its training data or current context.
Module 1 – Understanding GenAI
Human Judgment Boundary ▶
The line between tasks AI can safely assist with (drafting, organizing, rephrasing, exploring) and tasks that must remain human-led (ethical decisions, grading, policy choices, high-stakes interpretation).
Module 2 – Iterative Collaboration
This boundary is not fixed; it depends on context, risk, and institutional norms.
Iterative Prompting ▶
Treating AI interaction as a stepwise process: refining prompts, asking follow-up questions, and revising outputs rather than expecting a single perfect response.
Module 2 – Iterative Collaboration
LLM (Large Language Model) ▶
A model trained on large text corpora to predict the next token in a sequence. LLMs are pattern-completion engines: they generate fluent language without human-like understanding.
Module 1 – Understanding GenAI
Memory (Model Memory) ▶
A feature in some tools that stores information about you or your preferences across sessions. It can support convenience and consistency but raises privacy and data-governance considerations.
Module 3 – Customization
Multimodal Model ▶
A model that can take in and/or generate more than one type of data (e.g., text, images, audio, sometimes video). Multimodal models enable workflows like analyzing documents, images, or charts directly.
Module 4 – Multimodal GenAI
Pattern Prediction ▶
The core mechanism of LLMs and related models: given previous tokens and context, predict the most likely next token. “Intelligence” is emergent from pattern-matching, not understanding or intent.
Module 1 – Understanding GenAI
Persistent Workspace / Project Folder ▶
A structured, durable environment where the model can access past conversations, files, and instructions. Ideal for complex projects that require consistency, but less suitable for quick, one-off experimentation.
Module 3 – Customization
Prompt Pattern / Template ▶
A reusable prompt structure for recurring tasks (e.g., “role, task, audience, format, constraints”). Patterns reduce cognitive load and produce more consistent, predictable outputs.
Module 3 – Customization
Prompt-Level Customization ▶
Shaping the model’s behavior within a single interaction by specifying roles, goals, examples, tone, and constraints directly in the prompt—without changing global settings.
Module 3 – Customization
RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation) ▶
A method where the system retrieves relevant documents or chunks you provide and then generates responses grounded in that retrieved material. This can reduce hallucinations and support document-based reasoning.
Module 3 – Customization
Reasoning Steps ▶
Asking the model to outline how it arrived at an answer (e.g., “show your steps” or “explain your reasoning”) rather than providing only the final result. Helps with transparency and quality control.
Module 2 – Iterative Collaboration
Scope of AI Assistance ▶
The defined boundaries of what parts of a task AI is allowed to handle (drafting, summarizing, reformatting) versus what stays firmly in human hands (evaluation, final decisions, accountability).
Module 2 – Iterative Collaboration
Temporary Chat / Clean-Slate Chat ▶
A conversation that does not draw on long-term memory or persistent customization. Best used for privacy-sensitive work, experimentation, or divergent thinking where you want minimal hidden context.
Module 3 – Customization
Token ▶
A unit of text used by language models—roughly a word or sub-word piece. Context limits, costs, and many technical constraints are expressed in tokens.
Module 1 – Understanding GenAI
Tool Ecosystem (Copilot, ChatGPT, Gemini, Claude, Firefly, NotebookLM, Illinois Chat, etc.) ▶
The set of AI tools used across the certificate, each with distinct strengths, licensing models, and data-handling practices—for example: drafting in Copilot, document-grounded work in NotebookLM, secure internal work in Illinois Chat, or image generation in Firefly.
Module 4 – Multimodal GenAI
Use Case Boundary ▶
A decision point for whether a given task should be:
  • Handled primarily by AI,
  • Handled primarily by humans, or
  • Handled collaboratively.
This boundary is central for responsible use in teaching, research, and administration.
Module 2 – Iterative Collaboration
Workflow Integration ▶
Embedding AI into existing processes—such as literature reviews, curriculum design, communications, or administrative planning—rather than treating AI as a separate, one-off chatbot activity.
Module 2 – Iterative Collaboration
A key indicator of fluency is when AI steps are deliberately placed at points in a workflow where they add value without replacing judgment.
