2. Conceptual Grounding: Three Layers of Customization

Build a mental model of what you are actually changing when you ‚Äúcustomize‚Äù AI.

Before you change any settings, you need a clear mental model of what you are actually changing. Customization happens at three different layers, each with different strengths and risks.

1. Prompt-Level Customization

+

You shape behavior inside a single conversation with roles, constraints, examples, and clear output formatting. This layer is:

  • Ephemeral ‚Äî it applies to the current exchange.
  • Low-risk ‚Äî you can immediately see and adjust results.
  • Foundational ‚Äî everything else builds on good prompting.

2. Persistent Personalization

+

You configure how the system behaves across conversations through memory, custom instructions, or saved templates. This can:

  • Reduce repetitive setup and explanation.
  • Stabilize tone and structure across outputs.
  • Introduce new privacy and bias considerations.

3. Structural Customization (Agents & Workflows)

+

You build dedicated configurations‚Äîsuch as custom GPTs or agent-like tools‚Äîfor recurring, well-defined tasks (e.g., syllabus review, feedback drafting). This layer is powerful but:

  • Requires clear inputs, outputs, and boundaries.
  • Can be overbuilt for simple problems.
  • Must be documented if shared with students or colleagues.
Self-check: Where is your biggest pain point right now?

Pick one:

  • Explaining the same context to AI over and over.
  • Getting wildly different tones and formats each time.
  • Recreating the same multi-step task every week.

If your main issue is repeated explanation, start with persistent personalization. If tone and format vary, focus on prompt patterns. If you keep rebuilding the same multi-step process, consider a lightweight custom GPT or workflow.

Mini-Discussion

  • Ask participants which layer they use most today.
  • Have them map one current use to these three layers.
  • Surface misunderstandings (e.g., ‚Äúmemory‚Äù vs. ‚Äúprompt history‚Äù).

3. Hands-On Lab: Prompt Patterns & Personalization

Apply the concepts by building your own reusable patterns and instruction sets.

Activity 1: Build a Prompt Pattern

Choose a recurring task (e.g., drafting assignment prompts, summarizing articles, generating quiz questions). Adapt this pattern:

You are an assistant helping me with: [type of work]. Audience: [who this is for]. Context: [course, level, discipline]. Task: [what you want done]. Constraints: - Tone: [tone you prefer] - Structure: [headings, bullets, length] - Criteria: [rubric elements, policies, or key requirements] Before you begin, ask me 2‚Äì3 clarifying questions to avoid guessing.
Try this:

Run your adapted prompt twice, with two different content examples.

  • What stayed consistent across both outputs?
  • Where did the model drift away from your constraints?
  • What one change to your pattern might tighten the behavior?

Activity 2: Draft Persistent Instructions

Now draft instructions that describe how AI should generally support your work.

4. Ethics, Privacy & Risk

Make sure your customizations don‚Äôt create new problems for students or colleagues.

Customization can make AI feel more ‚Äúexpert‚Äù than it really is. That can be helpful‚Äîbut also risky‚Äîespecially when student work or sensitive information is involved.

Questions to Ask First

  • What data am I asking the system to store or reuse?
  • Could any of it identify specific students or colleagues?
  • Would I be comfortable explaining this setup to my students?
  • Am I treating AI as an assistant, or as an unquestioned authority?

Safer Defaults

  • Avoid uploading identifiable student work into external tools.
  • Prefer institutionally supported, secure systems for sensitive data.
  • Document what your customization is allowed‚Äîand not allowed‚Äîto do.
  • Be explicit with students when AI is used in your workflow.

Scenarios

Scenario 1: Grading Assistant GPT

+

A faculty member uploads a full set of student essays to a custom GPT and asks it to suggest rubric-aligned feedback and grades.

  • Benefits: Faster feedback drafts, rubric consistency.
  • Risks: Privacy concerns, overreliance on AI scores, hidden bias.
  • Alternatives: Use anonymized excerpts; generate feedback stems, not grades.

Scenario 2: Policy Explainer GPT

+

A department builds a GPT from public-facing policy documents to answer student questions about procedures and deadlines.

  • Benefits: 24/7 access, reduced email load.
  • Risks: Out-of-date policies, overconfident answers, missing nuance.
  • Mitigations: Timestamp content, link to official pages, add ‚Äúmay not be current‚Äù disclaimer.
