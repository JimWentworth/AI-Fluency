Prompts & Temperature: The Foundation

15 min Core Concepts ChatGPT Copilot
‚ñº

Core Concepts

Prompt: The input text or instructions given to an AI model to guide its response or behavior. The quality and specificity of your prompt directly affects the quality of the AI's output.

Temperature: A parameter that controls randomness in text generation. Lower values (0.0-0.3) make output focused and deterministic‚Äîthe model will choose the most probable next words, resulting in consistent, predictable responses. Higher values (0.7-1.0) increase creativity and variability‚Äîthe model considers less probable options, producing more diverse and surprising outputs.

Important Note: In most AI chat interfaces like ChatGPT and Copilot, you don't directly control temperature‚Äîit's preset by the system. However, you can influence how the model behaves through your prompts. The model adjusts its randomness based on what you're asking for. When you request factual information (like explaining a scientific concept), it naturally uses lower temperature. When you request creative content (like brainstorming or writing), it naturally uses higher temperature.

Key Insight: Even with identical prompts, AI outputs will vary between generations. You cannot recreate the exact same response twice‚Äîan important consideration for academic integrity and reproducibility. This variation is larger for creative tasks and smaller for factual tasks.

ChatGPT Microsoft Copilot

Exercise: Observe Temperature Effects in Real Tools

  1. Test with a factual prompt (lower temperature behavior)

    Start with a prompt that requests factual, straightforward information:

    Explain quantum entanglement to an undergraduate physics student in 3 sentences.

    Click "regenerate" 3 times and observe: The core facts stay the same, but phrasing varies slightly. This is low-temperature behavior‚Äîconsistent information with minor stylistic changes.

  2. Test with a creative prompt (higher temperature behavior)
    Write a creative opening line for a research paper about climate change impacts on migration patterns.

    Regenerate this 3 times and observe: You get very different opening lines‚Äîsome formal, some dramatic, completely different framing. This is high-temperature behavior‚Äîthe model explores diverse possibilities.

  3. Experiment with prompt phrasing to influence temperature

    Try these variations and notice how outputs differ:

    Provide the standard definition of machine learning.

    vs.

    Brainstorm 5 creative ways to explain machine learning to a 10-year-old.

    The first prompt triggers lower temperature (consistent, standard answer). The second triggers higher temperature (varied, creative responses).

  4. Advanced: Test with ambiguous prompts
    Write an introduction for a paper on social media's effects.

    Regenerate several times. Because this prompt is ambiguous (creative writing? literature review? opinion piece?), you'll see high variation. Being specific reduces this variation.

How to Take Advantage of Temperature Variation

When You Want Consistency (Low Temperature Behavior)

Use prompts with these characteristics:

  • Request "standard," "technical," "precise," or "accurate" information
  • Ask for definitions, explanations, or step-by-step procedures
  • Be very specific about what you want
  • Example: "Provide the standard APA format for citing a journal article"

Best for: Technical documentation, grading rubrics, factual summaries, instructional content

When You Want Variety (High Temperature Behavior)

Use prompts with these characteristics:

  • Request "creative," "brainstorm," "innovative," or "multiple options"
  • Ask for alternatives, variations, or diverse perspectives
  • Use words like "imagine," "explore," "generate ideas"
  • Example: "Brainstorm 10 creative ways to teach statistical significance"

Best for: Brainstorming, generating multiple versions, creative writing, exploring possibilities

‚ö° Pro Strategy: Use Multiple Generations

Don't settle for the first response, especially for creative tasks. Hit "regenerate" 3-5 times and compare options. You might find that the 4th generation is perfect while the first three missed the mark. This is free variation‚Äîuse it!

Reflection Questions

  • When would you want consistent outputs in your work? (Consider: technical documentation, grading rubrics, instructional materials)
  • When would variation be beneficial? (Consider: brainstorming lesson plans, generating diverse exam questions, exploring different explanations)
  • How does output variation relate to academic integrity? If two students get different outputs from similar prompts, how do you evaluate originality?
  • What happens if you try to use a student's submitted text as a prompt‚Äîwill you get their exact text back? (Spoiler: Usually not, which makes simple "reverse prompt" detection unreliable)

Key Takeaways

  • You can't directly control temperature in ChatGPT/Copilot, but your prompt phrasing influences the model's behavior
  • Factual/technical prompts trigger more consistent responses; creative prompts trigger more variation
  • Words like "standard," "technical," "precise" encourage consistency; words like "creative," "brainstorm," "explore" encourage variety
  • Use the regenerate button strategically‚Äîgenerate multiple options for creative tasks
  • You cannot recreate exact AI outputs, which has important implications for academic integrity
  • Being specific in your prompts reduces unwanted variation and improves output quality

Identifying Hallucinations & Using RAG

20 min Core Concepts ChatGPT Perplexity NotebookLM
‚ñº

Core Concepts

Hallucinations: When AI models confidently generate false or fabricated information. This happens because large language models (LLMs) predict the next word based on patterns in their training data‚Äîthey don't access a truth database or verify facts. When the model lacks sufficient data on a topic, it fills in gaps with plausible-sounding but incorrect information. The model doesn't "know" it's wrong; it's simply completing patterns based on probability.

RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation): A two-step process that reduces hallucinations by combining search with generation. First, the system retrieves relevant information from external sources (like the web or uploaded documents). Second, it provides this retrieved information to the LLM as context when generating responses. This grounds the AI's answers in actual sources rather than relying solely on training patterns.

Key Difference: ChatGPT relies primarily on training patterns from its knowledge cutoff date, making it prone to hallucinations about recent events, obscure topics, or specific facts. Perplexity searches the web first and provides cited answers, significantly reducing hallucinations. NotebookLM only uses your uploaded documents, ensuring responses are grounded in your specific sources.

ChatGPT Perplexity NotebookLM

Exercise: Compare AI with and without RAG

  1. Ask ChatGPT about a recent, obscure study

    Test ChatGPT's tendency to hallucinate by asking about something specific and recent:

    Tell me about the 2023 study on [insert your research area] published in [insert specific journal]. What were the main findings?

    Observe: ChatGPT may confidently provide details about a study that doesn't exist, or conflate multiple studies.

  2. Ask the same question in Perplexity

    Now try the identical query in Perplexity, which uses RAG by searching the web first.

    Observe: Perplexity searches current sources and provides citations. If it can't find the study, it will say so rather than fabricating details.

  3. Compare the outputs

    Evaluate both responses based on:

    • Does it provide specific citations or sources?
    • Can you verify the information through the provided sources?
    • How confident does each response sound?
    • Which would you trust for academic work?
  4. Advanced: Test NotebookLM with your own documents

    Upload 3-5 research papers or course materials to NotebookLM, then ask questions about them. Notice how it only answers based on your uploaded content and provides specific citations to those documents.

  • RAG reduces hallucinations by retrieving and citing actual sources before generating responses
  • ChatGPT is best for creative/drafting tasks; Perplexity/NotebookLM are best for research and fact-checking
  • Always verify AI-generated facts, especially for academic or research purposes
  • Tools that provide citations (RAG-enabled) are generally more trustworthy for factual queries
  • NotebookLM's document-grounded approach ensures responses stay within your source material

Tokens & Context Windows: Understanding Limits

18 min Core Concepts Claude ChatGPT
‚ñº

Core Concepts

Tokens: Basic units of text that AI models process. Think of tokens as the "atoms" of language for AI‚Äîthey're roughly equivalent to words or word fragments. A typical English word is 1-2 tokens. For example, "cat" is 1 token, "running" is 1 token, but "extraordinary" might be 2 tokens (extra + ordinary). Numbers, punctuation, and spaces also count as tokens. This matters because AI models have limits on how many tokens they can process at once.

Context Window: The maximum amount of text (measured in tokens) that an AI model can consider at one time, including both your input (prompts, uploaded documents) and the model's output (responses). Think of it as the model's "working memory." Once you exceed the context window, the model starts "forgetting" earlier parts of the conversation. A larger context window means the model can handle longer documents, maintain longer conversations, and reference more information when generating responses.

Key Insight: Different AI tools have dramatically different context windows. ChatGPT handles ~128,000 tokens (~96,000 words or ~300 pages). Claude handles ~200,000 tokens (~150,000 words or ~500 pages). This difference matters when working with long documents, extensive conversations, or complex research tasks.

Claude ChatGPT

Exercise: Test Context Window Limits

  1. Test with a short document (2-3 pages)

    Upload or paste a short research paper, article, or document into ChatGPT or Claude.

    Summarize the main argument and key findings of this document in 3 paragraphs. Then identify any methodological limitations discussed by the author.

    Observe: The AI should handle this easily and reference specific sections accurately.

  2. Test with a longer document (10-20 pages)

    Upload a longer paper, book chapter, or report.

    How does the argument in section 2 connect to the conclusions in the final section? Provide specific examples from both sections.

    Observe: Does the AI maintain accuracy across different sections? Can it connect ideas from the beginning and end?

  3. Test with an extremely long document (50+ pages) in Claude

    Upload a dissertation chapter, book, or comprehensive report to Claude (which has a larger context window).

    Create a detailed outline of this document's structure, including all major sections and their key points. Then identify the 3 most important arguments made across the entire document.

    Observe: Even with a large context window, very long documents may lead to the AI emphasizing recent sections over earlier ones.

  4. Advanced: Test conversation length limits

    Have an extended conversation with multiple back-and-forth exchanges (10+ turns). Then ask the AI to reference something from your first message. Does it remember accurately, or has it "forgotten" earlier context?

Understanding Context Window Trade-offs

When Context Window Size Matters

Choose tools with larger context windows when:

  • Analyzing long documents: Research papers, books, dissertations, comprehensive reports
  • Comparing multiple sources: Literature reviews requiring synthesis across many papers
  • Extended conversations: Multi-turn dialogues where context from early exchanges matters
  • Complex projects: Tasks requiring the AI to reference many different pieces of information

Example: Claude's 200K token window can handle an entire PhD dissertation (~500 pages) in one conversation, while ChatGPT's 128K window might require breaking it into chunks.

‚ö° Pro Strategy: Chunking for Better Results

Even with large context windows, breaking complex analysis into focused questions often yields better results than asking the AI to process everything at once. Instead of "Analyze this 300-page book," try:

  • "What are the main arguments in chapters 1-3?"
  • "How does the author's methodology in chapter 4 address the limitations identified earlier?"
  • "Synthesize the key findings from chapters 5-7 and explain how they support the thesis."

This approach works better because it directs the AI's attention to specific sections rather than trying to "see" the entire document at once.

Reflection Questions

  • What types of documents or tasks in your work would benefit from larger context windows?
  • Have you ever had an AI "forget" earlier parts of a conversation? What was happening with the context window?
  • When might it be better to break a task into smaller chunks rather than using a large context window?
  • How would you explain to students why an AI might give different answers when you upload a document all at once vs. in sections?

Key Takeaways

  • Tokens are the basic units AI models process‚Äîroughly 1.3 tokens per English word on average
  • Context windows limit how much text an AI can "see" at once, including both input and output
  • Different tools have different context window sizes‚Äîchoose accordingly for your task
  • Larger context doesn't always mean better understanding‚Äîfocused questions often work better
  • When working with very long documents, expect the AI to emphasize recent sections more than earlier ones
  • The context window includes your conversation history‚Äîlong conversations can "fill up" the window
