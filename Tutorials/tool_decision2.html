Rule of Thumb

For exploration and low-risk drafting: ChatGPT, Gemini, and Copilot are all appropriate under the campus agreement.

For internal, consequential, or sensitive work: start with Illinois Chat. Use commercial tools only when you are sure the data is permitted and the risk is low.

Microsoft Copilot Microsoft 365

Campus / licensed Personal account

  • Best for: Help inside Outlook, Word, PowerPoint, Excel, and Teams.
  • Campus version: Runs under the institutional agreement, using your Illinois account and respecting campus data protections.
  • Personal Microsoft or free accounts: May not have the same protections; avoid using them for internal or student-related work.
  • Easy starting point: Try “summarize this email thread” or “turn these bullet notes into a draft message” directly inside the app you’re already using.
ChatGPT vs ChatGPT Edu OpenAI

ChatGPT Edu (campus) Public ChatGPT

  • ChatGPT Edu: Campus-licensed environment with stronger privacy controls and data protections suitable for instructional and many research-adjacent workflows.
  • Public ChatGPT: A personal account that is not covered by campus agreements; treat it like any other public web service.
  • Best for: Brainstorming, drafting, revising, structured reasoning, and multimodal experimentation.
  • Easy starting point: Use a low-stakes prompt like “Help me outline a 3–5 point email explaining X to Y audience” and then edit the output yourself.
Gemini and Google Workspace Google

Campus Gemini / Workspace Consumer Gemini

  • Best for: Work connected to Google tools—Docs, Sheets, Slides, Gmail, and Drive.
  • Campus-linked Gemini: When tied to your Illinois Google account, it operates within the institutional agreement for covered use-cases.
  • Consumer Google accounts: Not covered by campus protections; avoid using those accounts for internal or student-related content.
  • Easy starting point: In Docs or Gmail, try “suggest a clearer version of this paragraph” or “generate three subject line options” using Gemini’s built-in prompts.
Illinois Chat Campus-hosted

Illinois-hosted

  • Best for: Anything involving institutional policies, student scenarios, HR-adjacent topics, or other internal documents.
  • Data handling: Designed to keep data within campus infrastructure and aligned with the highest privacy expectations.
  • Good use-cases: Drafting policy language, rephrasing internal memos, summarizing committee notes, exploring options for sensitive communications.
  • Easy starting point: Paste non-identifiable excerpts from an internal document and ask, “Summarize the key decisions and open questions for a brief update to my department.”
Other Tools (e.g., Perplexity) Web-based

Public web tools

  • Examples: Perplexity and similar “answer engine” tools that blend web search with AI responses.
  • Low-friction use: Some of these tools allow limited use with minimal setup, which makes them appealing as quick, informal starting points.
  • Important distinction: These tools are not covered by campus data agreements. Treat them like any other public website.
  • Safe use-cases: General curiosity, background reading, or exploring public topics where you would be comfortable using a normal search engine.
  • Not for: Student data, internal planning, unpublished research, or anything you wouldn’t post on a public web form.
