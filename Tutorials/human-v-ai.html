Case Study: When to Use AI vs. Human Judgment

Scenario: You are Dr. Rivera, designing and running a new upper-level assignment. You want to use AI to save time and spark ideas—but you also want to protect student learning, equity, and academic integrity.

At each step, choose how you’ll collaborate with AI. After each decision, you’ll see feedback on whether AI should support, or human expertise should lead.

Guiding question: Is this task exploratory and reversible, or consequential and value-laden?

Decision 1: Framing the Assignment

You have a rough idea: a research-based project where students analyze a real-world problem. You’re starting from a blank page.

How will you use AI here?

Decision 1 Outcome: AI Drafts the Assignment

Reflection: This uses AI in a high-influence, low-judgment way. You gain speed, but you risk unclear expectations, misaligned outcomes, and generic prompts.

Key idea: Assignment design is where human expertise should lead. AI can help with wording and examples, but it shouldn’t define what students will learn.

Decision 1 Outcome: Human Leads, AI Refines

Reflection: This is a strong human → AI → human loop. You define the goals and structure, then use AI for clarity, examples, and alternative framings.

Key idea: Use AI for exploration and language, not to decide what the assignment is about.

Decision 1 Outcome: No AI Used

Reflection: This is safe, but underuses AI’s strengths. For low-risk, exploratory drafting and revising, AI can help you see options you might not consider on your own.

Key idea: The question is not “AI or no AI,” but “Where can AI help without taking over judgment?”

Decision 2: Designing the Rubric

The assignment description is ready. Now you need a rubric to assess student work.

How will you use AI for the rubric?

Decision 2 Outcome: AI-Generated Rubric

Reflection: Assessment is consequential for students. If AI defines the rubric, it’s effectively deciding what “good” looks like.

Key idea: Rubrics are value-laden. AI can suggest criteria, but human judgment must define and approve them.

Decision 2 Outcome: Human Criteria, AI Refinement

Reflection: This keeps human expertise at the center. You decide what matters; AI helps you express it clearly and consistently.

Key idea: Use AI to support clarity, not to determine standards.

Decision 2 Outcome: AI Brainstorm, Human Rebuilds

Reflection: This can work, but beware of anchoring on AI’s first draft. It’s better to start from your own non-negotiable criteria, then compare with AI’s ideas.

Key idea: AI is best as a brainstorming partner, not the source of truth.

Decision 3: Giving Feedback on a Student Draft

A student submits a first draft. You’re busy, and AI feedback tools look tempting.

How will you involve AI?

Decision 3 Outcome: AI as Main Feedback Voice

Reflection: Feedback is relational and consequential. Outsourcing it to AI risks privacy issues, generic comments, and misalignment with your rubric.

Key idea: AI should never be the primary voice giving feedback to students.

Decision 3 Outcome: Human Diagnosis, AI Phrasing

Reflection: You use human judgment to decide what matters, and AI to help with wording and consistency across many students.

Key idea: This is a strong pattern: human decides, AI assists with expression.

Decision 3 Outcome: AI Extends Bandwidth

Reflection: AI helps you process a complex draft, but you retain full ownership of evaluative comments.

Key idea: This is an example of using AI to expand cognitive bandwidth without outsourcing judgment.

Decision 4: Revising the Assignment for Next Term

You’ve finished the term and collected anonymized samples of student work plus your own reflections. You want to improve the assignment for next time.

How will you use AI now?

Decision 4 Outcome: Pattern Detection with AI

Reflection: AI helps you see patterns in student work you might miss. You still decide which issues matter and what to change.

Key idea: This is a good use of AI for exploratory analysis.

Decision 4 Outcome: AI Redesigns the Assignment

Reflection: Allowing AI to redesign the assignment from the ground up hands over too much pedagogical control.

Key idea: Assignment format and structure should be grounded in your goals, students, and context. AI can suggest options, but you choose.

Decision 4 Outcome: Human Reflection, AI Options

Reflection: You bring reflective judgment from your own class; AI helps translate that into concrete, testable improvements.

Key idea: This balances human insight with AI’s ability to generate variations quickly.

Summary: What Did You Notice About AI vs. Human Judgment?

  • Good fits for AI: drafting options, clarifying language, reorganizing content, spotting patterns, and generating examples.
  • Good fits for human judgment: defining learning goals, setting standards, grading, giving feedback, and making ethical or relational decisions.
  • Core heuristic: Use AI for exploration and bandwidth; rely on human expertise for consequential and value-laden decisions.

Reflection prompt: Where in your work do you see a similar boundary between “AI helps” and “human must lead”?
