Personalizing Your AI Workspace

Using ChatGPT as an example, with principles that carry across tools like Gemini, Claude, and Copilot.

ⓘ Features change fast — the categories stay useful.

Why this matters

Modern AI tools let you tune how they behave, what they remember, and how they fit into your teaching, learning, and research. This page highlights eight capability areas using ChatGPT as the concrete example, while encouraging you to look for the same patterns in any platform you use.

Use personalization to reduce friction and extend your thinking — not to outsource judgment.

Explore the 8 areas

What this is (ChatGPT): A persistent profile where you describe:

  • Your role, discipline(s), and context (e.g., “faculty in history,” “graduate student in CS”).
  • How you want feedback (skeptical, encouraging, concise, step-by-step).
  • Formatting preferences (bullets vs. prose, reading level, citation style).

What to look for in other tools: Any settings labeled “preferences,” “profile,” “style,” or “system instructions.”

Why this matters: Reduces repetitive setup and aligns the AI with your working style, especially useful for recurring tasks like assignment design or email drafts.

Check yourself: If your instructions are too narrow (“always write like X”), you may limit creative or divergent responses.

What this is (ChatGPT): An optional feature that lets the model remember recurring facts about your work and preferences across conversations.

Use it for:

  • Ongoing course or project builds (e.g., “this is for my GEN AI certificate program”).
  • Stable preferences (e.g., “always push back on my first idea”).
  • Common constraints (e.g., “assume FERPA-compliant scenarios”).

What to look for in other tools: Options like “memory,” “remember this,” or “workspace history.”

Privacy check: Treat memory like a semi-public whiteboard. Don’t store real student data, confidential HR details, or anything that needs strict access control.

What this is (ChatGPT): Projects let you bundle files, instructions, and chats into a single context for a course, grant proposal, or research topic.

Use it for:

  • Course design spaces (syllabi, assignments, rubrics in one place).
  • Research or grant proposals with multiple drafts and source documents.
  • Administrative workflows (policies, templates, communications).

What to look for in other tools: “Projects,” “notebooks,” “workspaces,” or “collections.”

Watch out for: Context drift. Re-check which workspace you’re in before asking a high-stakes question.

What this is (ChatGPT): Custom GPTs that bake in instructions, example interactions, and sometimes tools, effectively creating a tailored assistant.

Use it for:

  • A course-specific “Assignment Design Assistant.”
  • A “UDL Reviewer” that checks course materials for accessibility prompts.
  • Department workflows (e.g., “Syllabus Formatter” aligned with local policies).

What to look for in other tools: Names like “agents,” “apps,” “AI workflows,” “copilots,” or “studios.”

Critical check: Decide when this should support a human (reviewing drafts) versus acting autonomously (auto-sending emails is usually a bad idea).

What this is: Ways to share chats, projects, or custom assistants with colleagues or students.

Use it for:

  • Faculty development: share a “prompt lab” or assignment builder GPT.
  • Program teams: co-develop policies, FAQs, or communications with a shared AI context.
  • Students: carefully designed, transparent helpers for brainstorming or self-check activities.

Risks to manage: Groupthink and hidden assumptions. Encourage at least one person to “play skeptic” with both the group and the AI.

What this is (ChatGPT examples):

  • Research – pulls in and cites external sources for information-seeking tasks.
  • Teach Me – guides you through material step-by-step, with questions and checkpoints.
  • File and data tools – analyze spreadsheets, PDFs, or datasets.

Parallel features in other tools: Gemini’s grounded search and NotebookLM, Claude’s document analysis, Copilot’s 365-context tools, etc.

Why this matters: These modes shift the AI from “just chatting” to structured research or learning flows. They are powerful, but you still need to:

  • Check how sources are selected and cited.
  • Cross-verify high-stakes claims in original documents.
  • Be explicit about when you’re exploring vs. confirming.

Key idea: Don’t memorize brand names. Look for these capability buckets in any tool:

  • Personalization: Custom instructions, profiles, system messages.
  • Persistence: Memory, history, long-lived workspaces.
  • Project spaces: Workspaces, notebooks, collections, artifacts.
  • Custom agents: GPTs, agents, copilots, workflows, apps.
  • Sidecar modes: Research, teaching, data analysis, multimodal tools.

Practical move: When a new tool appears, ask: “Where do I set its personality? Where does it remember things? How do I group work? Does it have a research/learning mode?”

What this is: “No-memory” or temporary chats where the AI does not store what you say for future use.

Use them for:

  • Brainstorming many different options or angles on a problem.
  • Seeing how the model responds without your usual instructions and history.
  • Testing radically different framings or tones for the same task.

Why this matters: Personalization makes the model more consistent, but it also narrows the space of ideas. Temporary chats intentionally remove those constraints so the AI can challenge your habits and surface alternatives you might not consider when it is tuned to your “normal” way of working.

Good habit: For any important decision or design, consider running at least one temporary chat to generate divergent options before committing.

1 Divergent Thinking & Temporary Chats

Temporary or “no-memory” chats are essential when you need divergent thinking — that is, idea generation unconstrained by your past preferences, writing style, or project history. Personalization improves consistency, but it also narrows the model’s creative search space. Starting a temporary chat clears those constraints, allowing the AI to explore alternative framings, challenge your assumptions, and surface options you’re less likely to think of when the system is tuned to “your way” of working.

Use these sessions for brainstorming, reframing problems, or testing radically different directions before returning to a personalized workspace.

Designed for: life-long learners (students, staff, and faculty)
