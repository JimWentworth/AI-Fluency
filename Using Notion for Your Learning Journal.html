Using Notion for Your Learning Journal - Quick Guide

A simple guide to help students track their learning journey in the AI Certificate program using Notion.

Getting Started with Notion

Step 1: Create Your Account

  • Visit notion.so and sign up for a free account
  • Download the desktop or mobile app (optional but recommended)

Step 2: Copy the Template (Recommended)

  • Visit the AI Fluency Journal Template
  • Click Duplicate in the top-right corner
  • The template will be copied to your workspace with all modules pre-organized
  • You can edit, add, or remove sections as you prefer

Step 3: Or Create Your Own from Scratch

  • Click + New Page in your sidebar
  • Give it a title like "AI Certificate Learning Journal"
  • Choose an icon and cover image to personalize it

Step 4: Structure Your Journal

If you're creating from scratch, here are two simple approaches:

Option A: Weekly Pages

  • Create a new page for each week
  • Use headings to organize by topic or day
  • Add bullet points for key learnings, questions, and reflections

Option B: Database Approach

  • Create a database with these properties:
    • Date - when you learned something
    • Topic - what you studied (AI concepts, tools, projects)
    • Key Learnings - main takeaways
    • Questions - things you want to explore further
    • Status - Not Started, In Progress, Completed
Tips for Effective Journaling

Daily Habit

  • Spend 5-10 minutes after each learning session to capture thoughts
  • Write while concepts are fresh in your mind

What to Include

  • New concepts and definitions you learned
  • Code snippets or examples that helped you understand
  • Connections between different topics
  • Challenges you faced and how you solved them
  • Resources you found helpful (links, videos, articles)
  • Personal insights and "aha" moments

Use Notion Features

  • Toggles - hide detailed notes to keep your page clean
  • Callouts - highlight important insights or questions
  • Code blocks - save code examples with syntax highlighting
  • Bookmarks - save helpful links with previews
  • Inline databases - create tables for tracking projects or resources

Four Alternatives to Notion

1. Google Docs

Best for: Students who want simplicity and are already in the Google ecosystem

Pros:

  • Extremely simple to use - no learning curve
  • Automatic saving and version history
  • Easy sharing with instructors or peers
  • Works on any device with a browser
  • Free with Google account
  • Familiar interface most students already know

Cons:

  • Limited organizational features for long-term journaling
  • Not as flexible as dedicated note-taking tools
  • Can become cluttered with many entries
  • Fewer formatting options than specialized tools

2. Adobe Express (formerly Adobe Spark)

Best for: Students who want a visual, creative approach to their learning journal

Pros:

  • Beautiful templates for creating visual journal entries
  • Great for incorporating images, graphics, and design elements
  • Can create web pages or PDFs from your journal
  • Free tier available with Adobe account
  • Easy to share polished versions with others
  • Perfect for students who think visually

Cons:

  • More focused on presentation than quick note-taking
  • Can be overkill if you just want text notes
  • Requires internet connection
  • May take more time to make entries look good
  • Learning curve for design features

3. Day One

Best for: Students who want a dedicated, polished journaling experience

Pros:

  • Specifically designed for journaling with beautiful interface
  • Automatic prompts and reminders to encourage consistent journaling
  • Excellent search and tagging system
  • End-to-end encryption for privacy
  • Photo integration and location tracking
  • Available on Mac, iOS, Android, and web

Cons:

  • Premium features require subscription (around $35/year)
  • Less flexible than general note-taking apps
  • Not ideal for collaborative work or sharing with instructors
  • Can't easily embed code snippets or technical content

4. Journey

Best for: Students who want a cross-platform journaling app with multimedia features

Pros:

  • Works across all platforms (Windows, Mac, Linux, iOS, Android, web)
  • Supports photos, videos, audio recordings, and drawings
  • Google Drive and Google Photos integration
  • Mood tracking and weather automatically logged
  • Export options to PDF, DOCX, or JSON
  • Free version available with good features

Cons:

  • Premium features require subscription
  • Can feel overwhelming with all the extra features
  • Interface not as polished as Day One
  • May be too diary-focused for technical learning journals

Final Recommendation

The best tool is the one you'll actually use consistently. Start simple - whether you choose Notion or an alternative, begin with basic entries and add complexity as needed. The goal is to capture your learning journey, not to create perfect notes.
